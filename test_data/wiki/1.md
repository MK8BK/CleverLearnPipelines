Portugal
========

[Coordinates](/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system"): [39°N 8°W﻿ / ﻿39°N 8°W﻿ / 39; -8](https://geohack.toolforge.org/geohack.php?pagename=Portugal&params=39_N_8_W_type:country)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Country in Southwestern Europe

For other uses, see [Portugal (disambiguation)](/wiki/Portugal_(disambiguation) "Portugal (disambiguation)").

**Portugal**,[[f]](#cite_note-20) officially the **Portuguese Republic**,[[g]](#cite_note-21) is a country in the [Iberian Peninsula](/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") in [Southwestern Europe](/wiki/Southwestern_Europe "Southwestern Europe"). Featuring [the westernmost point](/wiki/Cabo_da_Roca "Cabo da Roca") in [continental Europe](/wiki/Continental_Europe "Continental Europe"), Portugal borders [Spain](/wiki/Spain "Spain") to its north and east, with which it shares [the longest uninterrupted border](/wiki/Portugal-Spain_border "Portugal-Spain border") in the [European Union](/wiki/European_Union "European Union"); to the south and the west is the [North Atlantic Ocean](/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean "North Atlantic Ocean"); and to the west and southwest lie the [Macaronesian archipelagos](/wiki/Macaronesia "Macaronesia") of the [Azores](/wiki/Azores "Azores") and [Madeira](/wiki/Madeira "Madeira"), which are the two [autonomous regions of Portugal](/wiki/Autonomous_Regions_of_Portugal "Autonomous Regions of Portugal"). [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon") is the [capital](/wiki/Capital_city "Capital city") and [largest city](/wiki/List_of_largest_cities_in_Portugal "List of largest cities in Portugal"), followed by [Porto](/wiki/Porto "Porto"), which is the only other [metropolitan area](/wiki/Metropolitan_areas_in_Portugal "Metropolitan areas in Portugal").

The western part of the Iberian Peninsula has been continuously inhabited since [prehistoric times](/wiki/Prehistoric_Iberia "Prehistoric Iberia"), with the earliest signs of [settlement](/wiki/Human_settlement "Human settlement") dating to 5500 BC.[[15]](#cite_note-CIA-22) [Celtic](/wiki/Celts "Celts") and [Iberian](/wiki/List_of_the_Pre-Roman_peoples_of_the_Iberian_Peninsula "List of the Pre-Roman peoples of the Iberian Peninsula") peoples arrived in the first millennium BC. The region came under [Roman](/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") control in the second century BC, followed by a succession of [Germanic peoples](/wiki/Germanic_peoples "Germanic peoples") and the [Alans](/wiki/Alans "Alans") from the fifth to eighth centuries AD. [Muslims invaded](/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_the_Iberian_Peninsula "Muslim conquest of the Iberian Peninsula") mainland Portugal in the eighth century, but were gradually expelled by the Christian [*Reconquista*](/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Reconquista "Portugal in the Reconquista") culminating with the capture of the [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve,_Portugal "Algarve, Portugal"), between 1238 and 1249 AD.[[16]](#cite_note-23)[[17]](#cite_note-24) Modern Portugal began taking shape during this period, initially as a [county](/wiki/County_of_Portugal "County of Portugal") of the Christian [Kingdom of León](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n "Kingdom of León") in 868 and recognized unequivocally as a [kingdom](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal "Kingdom of Portugal") with *[Manifestis Probatum](/wiki/Manifestis_Probatum "Manifestis Probatum")* in 1179.[[18]](#cite_note-Jenkins_Sofos_2023_p145-25)

As one of the earliest participants in the [Age of Discovery](/wiki/Age_of_Discovery "Age of Discovery"), Portugal made several advancements in [nautical science](/wiki/Portuguese_nautical_science "Portuguese nautical science"), the discovery of the *[volta do mar](/wiki/Volta_do_mar "Volta do mar")*, which unlocked safe round-trip voyages enabling deep-sea travel, stands out as a major turning point in world's history.[[h]](#cite_note-27) It settled Madeira and the Azores, and established itself as a major economic and political power, largely through a [maritime empire](/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") that extended mostly along the [South Atlantic](/wiki/South_Atlantic "South Atlantic") and [Indian Ocean](/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean") coasts.[[20]](#cite_note-28) The Portuguese [were among the first Europeans](/wiki/Portuguese_maritime_exploration "Portuguese maritime exploration") to explore and discover new territories and sea routes establishing a global commercial network of settlements, colonies, and trading posts.[[21]](#cite_note-29) A [dynastic crisis](/wiki/War_of_the_Portuguese_Succession "War of the Portuguese Succession") in the early 1580s resulted in the [Iberian Union](/wiki/Iberian_Union "Iberian Union") (1580–1640), which unified Portugal under Spanish rule,[[22]](#cite_note-30) marking its gradual decline as a global power.[[23]](#cite_note-31) Portuguese sovereignty was regained in 1640 and was followed by a [costly and protracted war](/wiki/Portuguese_Restoration_War "Portuguese Restoration War") lasting until 1688, while the [1755 Lisbon earthquake](/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake "1755 Lisbon earthquake") destroyed the city and further damaged the empire's economy.[[24]](#cite_note-32)

The [Napoleonic Wars](/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Napoleonic Wars") drove the [relocation of the court to Brazil](/wiki/Transfer_of_the_Portuguese_court_to_Brazil "Transfer of the Portuguese court to Brazil") in 1807, leading to its [elevation from colony to kingdom](/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Portugal,_Brazil_and_the_Algarves "United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil and the Algarves") which culminated with its [independence](/wiki/Independence_of_Brazil "Independence of Brazil") in 1822,[[25]](#cite_note-33) triggering a [civil war](/wiki/Liberal_Wars "Liberal Wars") (1828–1834) between supporters for a [constitutional monarchy](/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") and [absolutist monarchists](/wiki/Absolutism_(European_history) "Absolutism (European history)").[[26]](#cite_note-34) The monarchy endured until the [5 October 1910 revolution](/wiki/5_October_1910_revolution "5 October 1910 revolution"), which replaced it with the [First Republic](/wiki/First_Portuguese_Republic "First Portuguese Republic"). A phase of unrest ultimately led to its replacement with the authoritarian [Ditadura Nacional](/wiki/Ditadura_Nacional "Ditadura Nacional") and then turned into the [Estado Novo](/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal) "Estado Novo (Portugal)").[[27]](#cite_note-35) Democracy was restored in 1974 following the [Carnation Revolution](/wiki/Carnation_Revolution "Carnation Revolution"), which brought an end to the [Portuguese Colonial War](/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War "Portuguese Colonial War") and allowed the last of Portugal's African territories to achieve independence.[[28]](#cite_note-36)

Portugal's imperial history has left a [vast cultural legacy](/wiki/Evolution_of_the_Portuguese_Empire "Evolution of the Portuguese Empire"), with around 300 million [Portuguese speakers](/wiki/Lusophone "Lusophone") around the world. Today, it is a [developed country](/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country") with an [advanced economy](/wiki/IMF_advanced_economy_list "IMF advanced economy list") relying chiefly upon services, industry, and tourism. Portugal, a member of the [United Nations](/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), the European Union, the [Schengen Area](/wiki/Schengen_Area "Schengen Area"), and the [Council of Europe](/wiki/Council_of_Europe "Council of Europe"), was one of the founding members of [NATO](/wiki/NATO "NATO"), the [eurozone](/wiki/Eurozone "Eurozone"), the [OECD](/wiki/OECD "OECD"), and the [Community of Portuguese Language Countries](/wiki/Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries "Community of Portuguese Language Countries").

Etymology
---------

The word *Portugal* derives from the combined [Roman](/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome")-[Celtic](/wiki/Celtic_languages "Celtic languages") place name [Portus Cale](/wiki/Portus_Cale "Portus Cale")[[29]](#cite_note-37)[[30]](#cite_note-38) (present-day's [conurbation](/wiki/Conurbation "Conurbation") of [Porto](/wiki/Porto "Porto") and [Vila Nova de Gaia](/wiki/Vila_Nova_de_Gaia "Vila Nova de Gaia")). Porto stems from the Latin for [port](/wiki/Port "Port"), *portus*; *Cale*'s meaning and origin is unclear. The mainstream explanation is an [ethnonym](/wiki/Ethnonym "Ethnonym") derived from the Callaeci, also known as the [Gallaeci](/wiki/Gallaeci "Gallaeci") peoples, who occupied the north-west of the [Iberian Peninsula](/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula").[[31]](#cite_note-Winicius-39) One theory proposes *Cale* is a derivation of the Celtic word for 'port'.[[32]](#cite_note-40) Another is that *Cala* was a Celtic goddess. Some French scholars believe it may have come from *Portus Gallus*,[[33]](#cite_note-41) the port of the Gauls.

Around 200 BC, the [Romans](/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") took Iberia from the [Carthaginians](/wiki/Punic_people "Punic people") during the [Second Punic War](/wiki/Second_Punic_War "Second Punic War"). In the process they conquered Cale, renaming it *Portus Cale* ('Port of Cale') and incorporating it into the province of [Gallaecia](/wiki/Gallaecia "Gallaecia"). During the [Middle Ages](/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), the region around Portus Cale became known by the [Suebi](/wiki/Suebi "Suebi") and [Visigoths](/wiki/Visigoths "Visigoths") as *Portucale*. The name *Portucale* changed into *Portugale* during the 7th and 8th centuries, and by the 9th century, it was used to refer to the region between the rivers Douro and [Minho](/wiki/Minho_River "Minho River"). By the 11th and 12th centuries, *Portugale*, *Portugallia*, *Portvgallo* or *Portvgalliae* was already referred to as *Portugal*.

History
-------

Main article: [History of Portugal](/wiki/History_of_Portugal "History of Portugal")

### Prehistory

Main article: [Prehistoric Iberia](/wiki/Prehistoric_Iberia "Prehistoric Iberia")

The region has been inhabited by humans since circa 400,000 years ago, when [Homo heidelbergensis](/wiki/Homo_heidelbergensis "Homo heidelbergensis") entered the area. The oldest human fossil found in Portugal is the 400,000-year-old *[Aroeira 3](/wiki/Aroeira_3 "Aroeira 3")* *H. Heidelbergensis* skull discovered in the [Cave of Aroeira](/wiki/Cave_of_Aroeira "Cave of Aroeira") in 2014.[[34]](#cite_note-42) Later [Neanderthals](/wiki/Neanderthal "Neanderthal") roamed the northern Iberian peninsula and a tooth has been found at Nova da Columbeira cave in [Estremadura](/wiki/Estremadura_Province_(1936%E2%80%9376) "Estremadura Province (1936–76)").[[35]](#cite_note-Birmp1-43) [Homo sapiens sapiens](/wiki/Homo_sapiens_sapiens "Homo sapiens sapiens") arrived in Portugal around 35,000 years ago and spread rapidly.[[36]](#cite_note-44) Pre-Celtic tribes inhabited Portugal. The [Cynetes](/wiki/Cynetes "Cynetes") developed a written language, leaving [stelae](/wiki/Stelae "Stelae"), which are mainly found in the south.

Early in the first millennium BC, several waves of [Celts](/wiki/Celts "Celts") arrived in Portugal from Central Europe and intermarried with the local populations to form several different ethnic groups. The [Celtic](/wiki/Celts "Celts") presence is patent in archaeological and linguistic evidence. They dominated most of northern and central Portugal, while the south maintained its older character (believed non-Indo-European, likely related to [Basque](/wiki/Basque_language "Basque language")) until the Roman conquest.[[37]](#cite_note-45) In southern Portugal, some small, semi-permanent commercial coastal outposts were also founded by [Phoenicians](/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") and [Carthaginians](/wiki/Carthaginian_Iberia "Carthaginian Iberia").

### Roman Portugal

Main articles: [Lusitania](/wiki/Lusitania "Lusitania"), [Gallaecia](/wiki/Gallaecia "Gallaecia"), and [Hispania](/wiki/Hispania "Hispania")

Romans first invaded the Iberian Peninsula in 219 BC. The Carthaginians, Rome's opponent in the [Punic Wars](/wiki/Punic_Wars "Punic Wars"), were expelled from their coastal colonies. During [Julius Caesar](/wiki/Julius_Caesar "Julius Caesar")'s rule, almost the entire peninsula was annexed to Rome. The conquest took two hundred years and many died, including those sentenced to work in slave mines or sold as slaves to other parts of the empire. Roman occupation suffered a setback in 155 BC, when a [rebellion](/wiki/Lusitanian_War "Lusitanian War") began in the north. The [Lusitanians](/wiki/Lusitanians "Lusitanians") and other native tribes, under the leadership of [Viriathus](/wiki/Viriathus "Viriathus"),[[38]](#cite_note-46)[[39]](#cite_note-47) wrested control of all of western Iberia. Rome sent legions to quell the rebellion but were unsuccessful. Roman leaders bribed Viriathus's allies to kill him in 139 BC; he was replaced by [Tautalus](/wiki/Tautalus "Tautalus").

In 27 BC, Lusitania gained the status of [Roman province](/wiki/Roman_province "Roman province"). Later, a northern province was separated from the province of [Tarraconensis](/wiki/Hispania_Tarraconensis "Hispania Tarraconensis"), under Emperor [Diocletian](/wiki/Diocletian "Diocletian")'s reforms, known as [Gallaecia](/wiki/Gallaecia "Gallaecia").[[40]](#cite_note-48) There are numerous ruins of castros ([hill forts](/wiki/Hill_forts "Hill forts")) and remains of the [Castro culture](/wiki/Castro_culture "Castro culture"), like the [Mozinho](/wiki/Castro_of_Monte_Mozinho "Castro of Monte Mozinho"), [Zambujal](/wiki/Castro_of_Zambujal "Castro of Zambujal"), [Cidadelhe](/wiki/Castro_of_Cidadelhe "Castro of Cidadelhe"), [Conímbriga](/wiki/Con%C3%ADmbriga "Conímbriga"), [Mirobriga](/wiki/Mir%C3%B3briga "Miróbriga"), [Briteiros](/wiki/Cit%C3%A2nia_de_Briteiros "Citânia de Briteiros") archaeological sites.

### Germanic kingdoms

In 409, with the decline of the [Roman Empire](/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"), the [Iberian Peninsula](/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") was occupied by [Germanic tribes](/wiki/Germanic_tribes "Germanic tribes").[[41]](#cite_note-49) In 411, with a federation contract with [Emperor Honorius](/wiki/Emperor_Honorius "Emperor Honorius"), many of these people settled in [Hispania](/wiki/Hispania "Hispania"). An important group was made up of the [Suebi](/wiki/Suebi "Suebi"), [Buri](/wiki/Buri_tribe "Buri tribe"),[[42]](#cite_note-50) [Vandals](/wiki/Vandals "Vandals") in [Gallaecia](/wiki/Gallaecia "Gallaecia"), who founded a [Suebi Kingdom](/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Suebi "Kingdom of the Suebi") with its capital in [Braga](/wiki/Braga "Braga"). They came to dominate central Portugal, including [Aeminium](/wiki/Aeminium "Aeminium") ([Coimbra](/wiki/Coimbra "Coimbra")) all the way to the [Tagus](/wiki/River_Tagus "River Tagus"), while the [Visigoths](/wiki/Visigoths "Visigoths") occupied the south.[[43]](#cite_note-Koller_Laitenberger_1998-51)
The [Suebi](/wiki/Suebi "Suebi") and the Visigoths were the Germanic tribes who had the most lasting presence in the territories corresponding to modern Portugal. As elsewhere in Western Europe, there was a sharp decline in urban life during the [Dark Ages](/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography) "Dark Ages (historiography)").[[44]](#cite_note-52)

Roman institutions disappeared in the wake of the [Germanic](/wiki/Germanic_peoples "Germanic peoples") invasions with the exception of [ecclesiastical](/wiki/Ecclesiastical "Ecclesiastical") organisations, which were fostered by the Suebi in the fifth century and adopted by the Visigoths afterwards. Although the Suebi and Visigoths were initially followers of [Arianism](/wiki/Arianism "Arianism") and [Priscillianism](/wiki/Priscillianism "Priscillianism"), they adopted [Catholicism](/wiki/Catholicism "Catholicism") from the local inhabitants. St. [Martin of Braga](/wiki/Martin_of_Braga "Martin of Braga") was a particularly influential evangelist.[[43]](#cite_note-Koller_Laitenberger_1998-51)

In 429, the Visigoths moved south to expel the [Alans](/wiki/Alans "Alans") and [Vandals](/wiki/Vandals "Vandals") and founded a kingdom with its capital in [Toledo](/wiki/Toledo,_Spain "Toledo, Spain"). From 470, conflict between the Suebi and Visigoths increased. In 585, the Visigothic King [Liuvigild](/wiki/Liuvigild "Liuvigild") conquered Braga and annexed Gallaecia; the [Iberian Peninsula](/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") was unified under a [Visigothic Kingdom](/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom "Visigothic Kingdom").[[43]](#cite_note-Koller_Laitenberger_1998-51) A new class emerged, unknown in Roman times: a [nobility](/wiki/Nobility "Nobility"), which played a key social and political role during the [Middle Ages](/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"). It was under the Visigoths that the Church began to play an important part within the state. As the Visigoths did not learn Latin from the local people, they had to rely on bishops to continue the Roman system of governance. The laws were made by councils of bishops, and the [clergy](/wiki/Clergy "Clergy") emerged as a high-ranking class.

### Islamic period

Today's continental Portugal, along with most of modern Spain, was invaded from the South and became part of [al-Andalus](/wiki/Al-Andalus "Al-Andalus") between 726 and 1249, following the [Umayyad Caliphate](/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate "Umayyad Caliphate") conquest of the [Iberian Peninsula](/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula"). This rule lasted decades in the North, up to five centuries in the South.[[45]](#cite_note-Britannica-53)

After defeating the [Visigoths](/wiki/Visigoths "Visigoths") in a few months, the Umayyad Caliphate started expanding rapidly in the peninsula. Beginning in 726, the land that is now Portugal became part of the vast Umayyad Caliphate's empire of [Damascus](/wiki/Damascus "Damascus"), until its collapse in 750. That year the west of the empire gained its independence under [Abd-ar-Rahman I](/wiki/Abd-ar-Rahman_I "Abd-ar-Rahman I") with the establishment of the [Emirate of Córdoba](/wiki/Emirate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba "Emirate of Córdoba"). The Emirate became the [Caliphate of Córdoba](/wiki/Caliphate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba "Caliphate of Córdoba") in 929, until its dissolution in 1031, into 23 small kingdoms, called [Taifa](/wiki/Taifa "Taifa") kingdoms.[[45]](#cite_note-Britannica-53)

The governors of the taifas proclaimed themselves [Emir](/wiki/Emir "Emir") of their provinces and established diplomatic relations with the Christian kingdoms of the north. Most of present-day Portugal fell into the hands of the [Taifa of Badajoz](/wiki/Taifa_of_Badajoz "Taifa of Badajoz") of the [Aftasid Dynasty](/wiki/Aftasid_Dynasty "Aftasid Dynasty"), and in 1022 the [Taifa of Seville](/wiki/Taifa_of_Seville "Taifa of Seville") of the [Abbadids](/wiki/Abbadids "Abbadids") poets. The Taifa period ended with the conquest of the [Almoravids](/wiki/Almoravids "Almoravids") in 1086, then by the [Almohads](/wiki/Almohads "Almohads") in 1147.[[46]](#cite_note-54)
Al-Andaluz was divided into districts called *Kura*. [Gharb Al-Andalus](/wiki/Gharb_Al-Andalus "Gharb Al-Andalus") at its largest consisted of ten kuras,[[47]](#cite_note-A._R._Disney,_p.55-55) each with a distinct capital and governor. The main cities were in the southern half of the country: [Beja](/wiki/Beja_(Portugal) "Beja (Portugal)"), [Silves](/wiki/Silves_Municipality,_Portugal "Silves Municipality, Portugal"), [Alcácer do Sal](/wiki/Alc%C3%A1cer_do_Sal "Alcácer do Sal"), [Santarém](/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m,_Portugal "Santarém, Portugal") and [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon"). The Muslim population consisted mainly of native Iberian converts to Islam and [Berbers](/wiki/Berbers "Berbers").[[48]](#cite_note-56) The Arabs (mainly noblemen from [Syria](/wiki/Syria "Syria")) although a minority, constituted the elite. The Berbers who joined them, were nomads from the [Rif Mountains](/wiki/Rif_Mountains "Rif Mountains") of North Africa.[[45]](#cite_note-Britannica-53)

Invasions from the North also occurred in this period, with [Viking](/wiki/Viking "Viking") incursions raiding the coast between the 9th and 11th centuries, including Lisbon.[[49]](#cite_note-57)[[50]](#cite_note-58) This resulted in the establishment of small [Norse](/wiki/Norsemen "Norsemen") settlements in the coastline between [Douro](/wiki/Douro "Douro") and [Minho](/wiki/Minho_Province "Minho Province").[[51]](#cite_note-59)

One likely Viking cultural heritage is found in the ancient *Marcas Poveiras* or [Siglas Poveiras](/wiki/Siglas_Poveiras "Siglas Poveiras"). It is generally accepted that the *siglas*, also known as *marcas*, are of [Scandinavian](/wiki/Scandinavian_people "Scandinavian people") origin. Based on numerous similarities initially found at the [Nationalmuseet](/wiki/National_Museum_of_Denmark "National Museum of Denmark") in [Copenhagen](/wiki/Copenhagen "Copenhagen"), several objects marked with "[home-marks](/wiki/Home-marks "Home-marks")" were also identified in [Funen](/wiki/Funen "Funen") (also known as [Fyn](/wiki/Fyn "Fyn")), [Denmark](/wiki/Denmark "Denmark"). Further studies revealed that the complex hereditary marking system of Póvoa de Varzim was also present in Fyn. Given the geographical distance, historical Viking incursions along the Portuguese coast, and pagan rituals practiced by people from this particular fishing region, it is believed that there is a partial Norse ancestry and cultural legacy.[[52]](#cite_note-60)

### Reconquista

Main article: [Portugal in the Reconquista](/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Reconquista "Portugal in the Reconquista")

The [Reconquista](/wiki/Reconquista "Reconquista") was a period when Christians reconquered the Iberian Peninsula from [Moorish](/wiki/Moors "Moors") domination. An Asturian Visigothic noble named [Pelagius of Asturias](/wiki/Pelagius_of_Asturias "Pelagius of Asturias") was elected leader in 718[[53]](#cite_note-61) by many of the ousted [Visigoth](/wiki/Visigoth "Visigoth") nobles. Pelagius called for the remnants of the Christian Visigothic armies to rebel against the Moors and regroup in the unconquered northern Asturian highlands, known today as the [Cantabrian Mountains](/wiki/Cantabrian_Mountains "Cantabrian Mountains"), in north-west Spain.[[54]](#cite_note-V._Livermore,_1969_pp._32-33-62) After defeating the Moors in the [Battle of Covadonga](/wiki/Battle_of_Covadonga "Battle of Covadonga") in 722, Pelagius was proclaimed king, thus founding the Christian [Kingdom of Asturias](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias "Kingdom of Asturias") and starting the war of Christian reconquest.[[54]](#cite_note-V._Livermore,_1969_pp._32-33-62)

At the end of the 9th century, the region of Portugal between the rivers Minho and Douro was reconquered from the Moors by nobleman and knight [Vímara Peres](/wiki/V%C3%ADmara_Peres "Vímara Peres") on the orders of King [Alfonso III of Asturias](/wiki/Alfonso_III_of_Asturias "Alfonso III of Asturias").[[55]](#cite_note-63) Finding many towns deserted, he decided to repopulate and rebuild them.[[56]](#cite_note-QuidNovi-64)

Vímara Peres elevated the region to the status of [County](/wiki/County "County"), naming it the [County of Portugal](/wiki/County_of_Portugal "County of Portugal") after its major port city – *Portus Cale* or modern [Porto](/wiki/Porto "Porto"). One of the first cities he founded is Vimaranes, known today as [Guimarães](/wiki/Guimar%C3%A3es "Guimarães") – "birthplace of the Portuguese nation" or the "cradle city".[[56]](#cite_note-QuidNovi-64)

After annexing the County of Portugal into one of the counties that made up the [Kingdom of Asturias](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias "Kingdom of Asturias"), King [Alfonso III of Asturias](/wiki/Alfonso_III_of_Asturias "Alfonso III of Asturias") knighted Vímara Peres, in 868, as the First Count of Portus Cale (Portugal). The region became known as *Portucale*, *Portugale*, and simultaneously *Portugália*.[[56]](#cite_note-QuidNovi-64) With the forced abdication of Alfonso III in 910, the Kingdom of Asturias split into three separate kingdoms; they were reunited in 924 under the crown of [León](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n "Kingdom of León").

In 1093 [Alfonso VI of León](/wiki/Alfonso_VI_of_Le%C3%B3n "Alfonso VI of León") bestowed the county to Henry of Burgundy and married him to his daughter, [Teresa of León](/wiki/Theresa,_Countess_of_Portugal "Theresa, Countess of Portugal"). Henry thus became [Henry, Count of Portugal](/wiki/Henry,_Count_of_Portugal "Henry, Count of Portugal") and based his newly formed county from Bracara Augusta (modern [Braga](/wiki/Braga "Braga")).

### Independence

Main article: [Portugal in the Middle Ages](/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Middle_Ages "Portugal in the Middle Ages")

At the [Battle of São Mamede](/wiki/Battle_of_S%C3%A3o_Mamede "Battle of São Mamede"), in the outskirts of [Guimarães](/wiki/Guimar%C3%A3es "Guimarães"), in 1128, [Afonso Henriques](/wiki/Afonso_I_of_Portugal "Afonso I of Portugal"), Count of Portugal, defeated his mother [Countess Teresa](/wiki/Teresa,_Countess_of_Portugal "Teresa, Countess of Portugal") and her lover [Fernão Peres de Trava](/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_Peres_de_Trava "Fernão Peres de Trava"), establishing himself as sole leader of the [county](/wiki/County_of_Portugal "County of Portugal"). Afonso continued his father Henry of Burgundy's Reconquista wars. His campaigns were successful and in 1139, he obtained a victory in the [Battle of Ourique](/wiki/Battle_of_Ourique "Battle of Ourique"), so was proclaimed [King of Portugal](/wiki/King_of_Portugal "King of Portugal") by his soldiers. This is traditionally taken as the occasion when the County of Portugal became the independent [Kingdom of Portugal](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal "Kingdom of Portugal") and, in 1129, the capital city was transferred from Guimarães to Coimbra. Afonso was recognized as the first king of Portugal in 1143 by King [Alfonso VII of León](/wiki/Alfonso_VII_of_Le%C3%B3n "Alfonso VII of León"), and in 1179 by [Pope Alexander III](/wiki/Pope_Alexander_III "Pope Alexander III") as Afonso I of Portugal, with the [papal bull](/wiki/Papal_bull "Papal bull") *[Manifestis Probatum](/wiki/Manifestis_Probatum "Manifestis Probatum")*.

Afonso Henriques and his successors, aided by military [monastic orders](/wiki/Christian_monasticism "Christian monasticism"), continued pushing southwards against the Moors. In 1217, during the reign of King [Afonso II](/wiki/Afonso_II_of_Portugal "Afonso II of Portugal"), Portuguese troops, supported by [crusaders](/wiki/Crusades "Crusades") and others, retook [Alcácer do Sal](/wiki/Siege_of_Alc%C3%A1cer_do_Sal "Siege of Alcácer do Sal"), with further land conquests from the Moors during the reign of [Sancho II](/wiki/Sancho_II_of_Portugal "Sancho II of Portugal"), mainly the start of the [conquest of the Algarve](/wiki/Portuguese_conquest_of_the_Algarve "Portuguese conquest of the Algarve") in 1238. After internal tensions, Sancho II was deposed from the throne and his brother, [Afonso III](/wiki/Afonso_III_of_Portugal "Afonso III of Portugal"), became king in 1248.[[58]](#cite_note-66) A year later, in 1249, the Reconquista ended with the capture of the [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve"), in the aftermath of the [Siege of Faro](/wiki/Siege_of_Faro_(1249) "Siege of Faro (1249)"), and the last Moorish settlements were expelled. With minor readjustments, Portugal's territorial borders have remained the same, making it one of the oldest established nations in Europe.

After a conflict with the [kingdom of Castile](/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile "Kingdom of Castile"), [Denis of Portugal](/wiki/Denis_of_Portugal "Denis of Portugal") signed the [Treaty of Alcañices](/wiki/Treaty_of_Alca%C3%B1ices "Treaty of Alcañices") in 1297 with Ferdinand IV of Castile. This treaty established the border between the kingdoms of Portugal and Leon. During the reign of Denis, the first university in Portugal opened in Lisbon, on March 1st 1290, being then transferred to Coimbra in 1308.[[59]](#cite_note-67) Denis died in 1325 and was succeeded by his son, [Afonso IV](/wiki/Afonso_IV_of_Portugal "Afonso IV of Portugal"), who had a strained relationship with his father that nearly [ended in war](/wiki/Battle_of_Alvalade "Battle of Alvalade"). In 1355, in the aftermath of the assassination of [Inês de Castro](/wiki/In%C3%AAs_de_Castro "Inês de Castro"), [Peter's I](/wiki/Peter_I_of_Portugal "Peter I of Portugal") lover, a civil war broke between the King, Afonso IV, and his son, and heir, Peter, with peace only being achieved in 1357.[[60]](#cite_note-68)[[61]](#cite_note-69) The reigns of Denis, Afonso IV, and Peter I mostly saw peace with the other kingdoms of Iberia.

In 1348-49 Portugal, as with the rest of Europe, was devastated by the [Black Death](/wiki/Black_Death "Black Death").[[62]](#cite_note-70) In 1373, Portugal, during the reign of [Ferdinand I](/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Portugal "Ferdinand I of Portugal"), made an [alliance with England](/wiki/Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance "Anglo-Portuguese Alliance"), the oldest standing alliance in the world. This alliance was signed during the [Fernandine Wars](/wiki/Fernandine_Wars "Fernandine Wars"), a series of conflicts between Portugal and Castile over the right to the throne of Castile. This alliance would be reinforced with the signing of the [Treaty of Windsor](/wiki/Treaty_of_Windsor_(1386) "Treaty of Windsor (1386)") in 1386.

### Age of Discoveries

Main article: [Portuguese Empire](/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire")

In 1383 [John I of Castile](/wiki/John_I_of_Castile "John I of Castile") and [Beatrice of Portugal](/wiki/Beatrice_of_Portugal "Beatrice of Portugal"), the only surviving legitimate child of [Ferdinand I of Portugal](/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Portugal "Ferdinand I of Portugal"), claimed the throne of Portugal. John of Aviz, later [John I of Portugal](/wiki/John_I_of_Portugal "John I of Portugal"), led a revolt against this claim and defeated the Castilians in the [Battle of Aljubarrota](/wiki/Battle_of_Aljubarrota "Battle of Aljubarrota"), with the [House of Aviz](/wiki/House_of_Aviz "House of Aviz") becoming the ruling house. The new ruling dynasty led Portugal to the limelight of European politics and culture. They created and sponsored literature, such as a history of Portugal, by [Fernão Lopes](/wiki/Fern%C3%A3o_Lopes "Fernão Lopes").[[63]](#cite_note-71)[[64]](#cite_note-Carvalho_2014-72)[[65]](#cite_note-73)

Portugal spearheaded European exploration of the world and the [Age of Discovery](/wiki/Age_of_Discovery "Age of Discovery") under the sponsorship of [Prince Henry the Navigator](/wiki/Prince_Henry_the_Navigator "Prince Henry the Navigator"). Portugal explored the Atlantic, encountering the [Azores](/wiki/Azores "Azores"), [Madeira](/wiki/Madeira "Madeira"), and [Portuguese Cape Verde](/wiki/Portuguese_Cape_Verde "Portuguese Cape Verde"), which led to the first [colonisation](/wiki/Colonialism "Colonialism") movements. The Portuguese explored the [Indian Ocean](/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean"), established trade routes in most of southern Asia, and sent the first direct European maritime trade and diplomatic missions to China ([Jorge Álvares](/wiki/Jorge_%C3%81lvares "Jorge Álvares")) and Japan ([Nanban trade](/wiki/Nanban_trade "Nanban trade")). In 1415, Portugal acquired its first colonies by conquering [Ceuta](/wiki/Ceuta "Ceuta"), in North Africa. Throughout the 15th century, [Portuguese explorers](/wiki/European_exploration_of_Africa#Portuguese "European exploration of Africa") sailed the coast of Africa, establishing trading posts [for commodities](/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal#Expansion_of_the_Portuguese_empire_(15th_and_16th_centuries) "Economic history of Portugal"), ranging from [gold](/wiki/Gold "Gold") to [slavery](/wiki/Slavery "Slavery"). Portugal sailed the [Portuguese India Armadas](/wiki/Portuguese_India_Armadas "Portuguese India Armadas") to Goa via the [Cape of Good Hope](/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope "Cape of Good Hope").

The [Treaty of Tordesillas](/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas "Treaty of Tordesillas") of 1494 was intended to resolve a dispute created following the return of [Christopher Columbus](/wiki/Christopher_Columbus "Christopher Columbus") and divided the newly located lands outside Europe between Portugal and Spain along a line west of the [Cape Verde](/wiki/Cape_Verde "Cape Verde") islands, off the west coast of Africa. In 1498 [Vasco da Gama](/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama "Vasco da Gama") became the first European to reach India by sea, bringing [economic prosperity to Portugal](/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal "Economic history of Portugal") and helping to start the [Portuguese Renaissance](/wiki/Portuguese_Renaissance "Portuguese Renaissance"). In 1500, the Portuguese explorer [Gaspar Corte-Real](/wiki/Gaspar_Corte-Real "Gaspar Corte-Real") reached what is now [Canada](/wiki/Canada "Canada") and founded the town of [Portugal Cove-St. Philip's](/wiki/Portugal_Cove-St._Philip%27s "Portugal Cove-St. Philip's"), one of many [Portuguese colonies of the Americas](/wiki/Portuguese_colonization_of_the_Americas "Portuguese colonization of the Americas").[[66]](#cite_note-74)[[67]](#cite_note-75)[[68]](#cite_note-76)

In 1500, [Pedro Álvares Cabral landed on Brazil](/wiki/Discovery_of_Brazil "Discovery of Brazil") and claimed it for Portugal.[[69]](#cite_note-77) Ten years later, [Afonso de Albuquerque](/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque "Afonso de Albuquerque") conquered [Goa](/wiki/Goa "Goa") in India, [Muscat](/wiki/Muscat,_Oman "Muscat, Oman") and [Ormuz](/wiki/Ormuz "Ormuz") in the [Persian Strait](/wiki/Persian_Strait "Persian Strait"), and [Malacca](/wiki/Malacca "Malacca"), now in Malaysia. Thus, the Portuguese empire held dominion over commerce in the Indian Ocean and South Atlantic. Portuguese sailors set out to reach Eastern Asia, landing in [Taiwan](/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan"), Japan, [Timor](/wiki/Timor "Timor"), [Flores](/wiki/Flores "Flores"), and the [Moluccas](/wiki/Moluccas "Moluccas"). Although it was believed the [Dutch](/wiki/Dutch_people "Dutch people") were the first Europeans to arrive in Australia, there is evidence the [Portuguese may have discovered it](/wiki/Theory_of_the_Portuguese_discovery_of_Australia "Theory of the Portuguese discovery of Australia") in 1521.[[70]](#cite_note-78)[[71]](#cite_note-79)[[72]](#cite_note-80)

Between 1519 and 1522 [Ferdinand Magellan](/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan "Ferdinand Magellan") organised a Spanish expedition to the [East Indies](/wiki/East_Indies "East Indies") which resulted in the first [circumnavigation](/wiki/Circumnavigation "Circumnavigation") of the globe. The [Treaty of Zaragoza](/wiki/Treaty_of_Zaragoza "Treaty of Zaragoza"), signed in 1529 between Portugal and Spain, divided the Pacific Ocean between Spain and Portugal.[[73]](#cite_note-81)

### Iberian Union and Restoration

Main article: [Iberian Union](/wiki/Iberian_Union "Iberian Union")

Portugal voluntarily entered a dynastic union (1580–1640) because the last two kings of the [House of Aviz](/wiki/House_of_Aviz "House of Aviz") ([Sebastian, King of Portugal](/wiki/Sebastian,_King_of_Portugal "Sebastian, King of Portugal"), followed by his grand-uncle [Henry, King of Portugal](/wiki/Henry,_King_of_Portugal "Henry, King of Portugal")) died without heirs, resulting in the [Portuguese succession crisis of 1580](/wiki/Portuguese_succession_crisis_of_1580 "Portuguese succession crisis of 1580"). [Philip II of Spain](/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain "Philip II of Spain") claimed the throne, under the pretext that his mother was a Portuguese princess, [Isabella of Portugal](/wiki/Isabella_of_Portugal "Isabella of Portugal"), the second child and first daughter of King [Manuel I of Portugal](/wiki/Manuel_I_of_Portugal "Manuel I of Portugal"). Philip II of Spain was accepted as Philip I of Portugal. Yet, Portugal did not lose its formal independence. Instead, a [union](/wiki/Iberian_Union "Iberian Union") of kingdoms was formed. But the joining of the two crowns deprived Portugal of an independent foreign policy, and led to its involvement in the [Eighty Years' War](/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War "Eighty Years' War") between Spain and the Netherlands.

War led to a deterioration of relations with [Portugal's oldest ally, England](/wiki/Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance "Anglo-Portuguese Alliance"), and the loss of [Hormuz](/wiki/Ormus "Ormus"), a strategic trading post located between [Iran](/wiki/Iran "Iran") and [Oman](/wiki/Oman "Oman"). From 1595 to 1663 the [Dutch Portuguese War](/wiki/Dutch-Portuguese_War "Dutch-Portuguese War") primarily involved Dutch companies invading Portuguese [colonies](/wiki/Colonies "Colonies") and commercial interests in Brazil, Africa, India and the Far East, resulting in the loss of Portugal's Indian sea trade monopoly.

In 1640 [John IV of Portugal](/wiki/John_IV_of_Portugal "John IV of Portugal") spearheaded an uprising backed by disgruntled nobles and was proclaimed king. The [Portuguese Restoration War](/wiki/Portuguese_Restoration_War "Portuguese Restoration War") ended the 60-year period of the Iberian Union under the [House of Habsburg](/wiki/House_of_Habsburg "House of Habsburg"). This was the beginning of the [House of Braganza](/wiki/House_of_Braganza "House of Braganza"), which reigned until 1910. [John V](/wiki/John_V_of_Portugal "John V of Portugal") saw a reign characterized by the influx of gold into the royal treasury, supplied largely by the [royal fifth](/wiki/Royal_fifth "Royal fifth") (tax on precious metals) from the [Portuguese colonies](/wiki/Portuguese_empire "Portuguese empire") of [Brazil](/wiki/State_of_Brazil "State of Brazil") and [Maranhão](/wiki/State_of_Maranh%C3%A3o_(colonial) "State of Maranhão (colonial)"). Most estimates place the number of Portuguese migrants to [Colonial Brazil](/wiki/Colonial_Brazil "Colonial Brazil") during the gold rush of the 18th century at 600,000.[[74]](#cite_note-ibge-82) This represented one of the largest movements of European populations to their colonies, during colonial times.

### Pombaline era and Enlightenment

Main article: [History of Portugal (1640–1777)](/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1640%E2%80%931777) "History of Portugal (1640–1777)")

In 1738 [Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo](/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_de_Carvalho_e_Melo,_1st_Marquis_of_Pombal "Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo, 1st Marquis of Pombal"), later ennobled as 1st [Marquis of Pombal](/wiki/Marquis_of_Pombal_(title) "Marquis of Pombal (title)"), began a career as the Portuguese Ambassador in London, later in Vienna. King [Joseph I](/wiki/Joseph_I_of_Portugal "Joseph I of Portugal") was crowned in 1750 and made him his [Minister](/wiki/Political_minister "Political minister") of Foreign Affairs. As the King's confidence in Carvalho e Melo increased, he entrusted him with more control of the state. By 1755, Carvalho e Melo was made prime minister. Impressed by British economic success witnessed as Ambassador, he successfully implemented similar [economic](/wiki/Economic_system "Economic system") policies in Portugal.

In 1761, during the reign of King José I, he banned the import of black slaves into mainland Portugal and India, not for humanitarian reasons, but because they were a necessary work force in Brazil. At the same time, he encouraged the trade of black slaves ("the pieces", in the terms of that time) to that colony, and with the support and direct involvement of the Marquis of Pombal, two companies were founded - the *Companhia do Grão-Pará e Maranhão* and the *Companhia Geral de Pernambuco e Paraíba* - whose main activity was the trafficking of slaves, mostly Africans, to Brazilian lands.[[75]](#cite_note-83)[[76]](#cite_note-84)

He reorganised the army and navy and ended legal discrimination against different [Christian](/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") sects.[[77]](#cite_note-85) He created companies and guilds to regulate commercial activity and one of the first [appellation systems](/wiki/Appellation "Appellation") by demarcating the region for production of [Port](/wiki/Port_wine "Port wine") to ensure the wine's quality. This was the first attempt to control wine quality and production in Europe. He imposed strict law upon all classes of Portuguese society, along with a widespread review of the tax system. These reforms gained him enemies in the upper classes.

The [1755 Lisbon earthquake](/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake "1755 Lisbon earthquake") devastated Portugal.

Lisbon was struck by a major [earthquake on 1 November 1755](/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake "1755 Lisbon earthquake"), magnitude estimated to have been between 7.7 and 9.0, with casualties ranging from 12,000 to 50,000.[[78]](#cite_note-86) Following the earthquake, Joseph I gave his prime minister more power, and Carvalho de Melo became an [enlightened despot](/wiki/Enlightened_absolutism "Enlightened absolutism"). In 1758 Joseph I was wounded in an attempted assassination. The [Marquis of Távora](/wiki/Marquis_of_T%C3%A1vora "Marquis of Távora"), several members of his family and even servants were tortured and executed in public with extreme brutality (even by the standards of the time), as alleged part of the [Távora affair](/wiki/T%C3%A1vora_affair "Távora affair").[[79]](#cite_note-87)[[80]](#cite_note-88)[[81]](#cite_note-89)

The following year, the [Jesuits](/wiki/Society_of_Jesus "Society of Jesus") were [suppressed and expelled](/wiki/Suppression_of_the_Society_of_Jesus "Suppression of the Society of Jesus"). This crushed opposition by publicly demonstrating even the aristocracy was powerless before Pombal. Further titled "Marquês de Pombal" in 1770, he ruled Portugal until Joseph I's death in 1777. The new ruler, Queen [Maria I of Portugal](/wiki/Maria_I_of_Portugal "Maria I of Portugal"), disliked Pombal because of his excesses, and upon her accession to the throne, withdrew all his political offices. Pombal was banished to his estate at [Pombal](/wiki/Pombal,_Portugal "Pombal, Portugal"), where he died in 1782.

Historians argue that Pombal's "enlightenment," while far-reaching, was primarily a mechanism for enhancing autocracy at the expense of individual liberty and especially an apparatus for crushing opposition, suppressing criticism, and furthering colonial exploitation and consolidating personal control, and profit.[[82]](#cite_note-90)

### Crises of the 19th century

Main articles: [History of Portugal (1777–1834)](/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1777%E2%80%931834) "History of Portugal (1777–1834)") and [History of Portugal (1834–1910)](/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1834%E2%80%931910) "History of Portugal (1834–1910)")

In 1807 Portugal refused [Napoleon](/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon")'s demand to accede to the [Continental System](/wiki/Continental_System "Continental System") of embargo against the United Kingdom; a French invasion under [General Junot](/wiki/Jean-Andoche_Junot "Jean-Andoche Junot") followed, and Lisbon was captured in 1807. British intervention in the [Peninsular War](/wiki/Peninsular_War "Peninsular War") helped maintain Portuguese independence; the last French troops were expelled in 1812.[[83]](#cite_note-91) During the [Napoleonic invasions](/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars "Napoleonic Wars"), the [Portuguese royal family transferred](/wiki/Transfer_of_the_Portuguese_court_to_Brazil "Transfer of the Portuguese court to Brazil") the court to [Rio de Janeiro](/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro "Rio de Janeiro"), in Brazil, making it the capital of the Portuguese empire between 1808 and 1821.[[84]](#cite_note-92)

In 1820, [constitutionalist](/wiki/Constitutionalist "Constitutionalist") insurrections took place at [Porto](/wiki/Porto "Porto") and Lisbon, and King John VI and his court were forced to return to mainland Portugal in 1821.[[85]](#cite_note-93) Lisbon regained its status as the capital of Portugal when Brazil declared its independence in 1822. The death of King [John VI](/wiki/John_VI_of_Portugal "John VI of Portugal") in 1826 led to a crisis of royal succession. His eldest son, [Pedro I of Brazil](/wiki/Pedro_I_of_Brazil "Pedro I of Brazil"), briefly became [Pedro IV of Portugal](/wiki/Pedro_IV_of_Portugal "Pedro IV of Portugal"), but neither the Portuguese nor Brazilians wanted a unified monarchy; consequently, Pedro abdicated the Portuguese crown in favor of his 7-year-old daughter, [Maria da Glória](/wiki/Maria_II_of_Portugal "Maria II of Portugal"), on the condition that when she came of age she would marry his brother, [Miguel](/wiki/Miguel_I_of_Portugal "Miguel I of Portugal"). Dissatisfaction at Pedro's constitutional reforms led the "absolutist" faction of landowners and the church to proclaim Miguel king in February 1828. This led to the [Liberal Wars](/wiki/Liberal_Wars "Liberal Wars"), also known as the *War of the Two Brothers* or the *Portuguese Civil War*, in which Pedro forced Miguel to abdicate and go into exile in 1834 and place his daughter on the throne as Queen [Maria II of Portugal](/wiki/Maria_II_of_Portugal "Maria II of Portugal").

After 1815 the Portuguese expanded their trading ports along the African coast, moving inland to take control of Angola and Mozambique. The slave trade was abolished in 1836. In [Portuguese India](/wiki/Portuguese_India "Portuguese India"), trade flourished in the colony of [Goa](/wiki/History_of_Goa "History of Goa"), with its subsidiary colonies of [Macau](/wiki/Portuguese_Macau "Portuguese Macau"), near Hong Kong, and [Timor](/wiki/Portuguese_Timor "Portuguese Timor"), north of Australia. The Portuguese successfully introduced Catholicism and the Portuguese language into their colonies, while most settlers continued to head to Brazil.[[86]](#cite_note-94)[[87]](#cite_note-95)

On 11 January 1890, the [British government](/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom "Government of the United Kingdom") delivered [an ultimatum](/wiki/1890_British_Ultimatum "1890 British Ultimatum") to Portugal, demanding the withdrawal of Portuguese forces from the area between Portugal's colonies of [Mozambique](/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique "Portuguese Mozambique") and [Angola](/wiki/Portuguese_Angola "Portuguese Angola"). The area had been claimed by Portugal as part of its colonialist [Pink Map](/wiki/Pink_Map "Pink Map") project, but Britain disputed these claims, mostly due to [Cecil Rhodes](/wiki/Cecil_Rhodes "Cecil Rhodes")' aspirations to create a [Cape to Cairo Railway](/wiki/Cape_to_Cairo_Railway "Cape to Cairo Railway"), which was intended to link all British colonies via a single railway. The government of Portugal quietly accepted the ultimatum and withdrew their forces from the disputed area, leading to a widespread backlash among the Portuguese public, who viewed acceptance of the British demands as a humiliation.[[88]](#cite_note-96)

### First Republic and Estado Novo

Main articles: [First Portuguese Republic](/wiki/First_Portuguese_Republic "First Portuguese Republic") and [Estado Novo (Portugal)](/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal) "Estado Novo (Portugal)")

On 1 February 1908, King [Carlos I](/wiki/Carlos_I_of_Portugal "Carlos I of Portugal") and his son and heir, [Luís Filipe, Prince Royal](/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Filipe,_Prince_Royal_of_Portugal "Luís Filipe, Prince Royal of Portugal"), were [assassinated](/wiki/Lisbon_Regicide "Lisbon Regicide") by Republican and [Carbonária](/wiki/Carbon%C3%A1ria "Carbonária") members.[[89]](#cite_note-97) Two years later, on 5 October 1910, a [coup d'état](/wiki/5_October_1910_revolution "5 October 1910 revolution") overthrew the near 800-year-old Monarchy and the Republic was proclaimed. During World War I, Portugal helped the [Allies](/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I") fight the [Central Powers](/wiki/Central_Powers "Central Powers"); however the war hurt its weak economy. Political instability and economic weaknesses were fertile ground for chaos and unrest during the [First Portuguese Republic](/wiki/First_Portuguese_Republic "First Portuguese Republic"). Several coups occurred during the First Republic, like the failed [Monarchy of the North](/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_North "Monarchy of the North") coup, but others had success, like the [December 1917 coup d'état](/wiki/December_1917_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "December 1917 coup d'état"), which led to the rise of [Sidónio Pais](/wiki/Sid%C3%B3nio_Pais "Sidónio Pais") to power. In 1921, the [*Bloody Night*](/wiki/Bloody_Night_(Lisbon,_1921) "Bloody Night (Lisbon, 1921)") revolt ended in the assassinaton of the Prime Minister and other high-ranking officials of the Republic.

These conditions led to the [28 May 1926 coup d'état](/wiki/28_May_1926_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "28 May 1926 coup d'état") and creation of the National Dictatorship (*[Ditadura Nacional](/wiki/Ditadura_Nacional "Ditadura Nacional")*). This in turn led to the right-wing dictatorship of the [Estado Novo](/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal) "Estado Novo (Portugal)") (New State), under [António de Oliveira Salazar](/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar "António de Oliveira Salazar") in 1933.

[António de Oliveira Salazar](/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar "António de Oliveira Salazar") ruled Portugal between 1932 and 1968, within the [Estado Novo](/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal) "Estado Novo (Portugal)") regime.

Paratroopers in a Portuguese Air Force helicopter during the [Portuguese Colonial War](/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War "Portuguese Colonial War")

Portugal remained neutral in [World War II](/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"). From the 1940s to 1960s, Portugal was a founding member of [NATO](/wiki/NATO "NATO"), [OECD](/wiki/OECD "OECD"), the [European Free Trade Association](/wiki/European_Free_Trade_Association "European Free Trade Association") (EFTA) and joined the [United Nations](/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") in 1955. New economic development projects and relocation of mainland Portuguese citizens into the overseas provinces in Africa were initiated, with [Angola](/wiki/Portuguese_Angola "Portuguese Angola") and [Mozambique](/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique "Portuguese Mozambique") being the main targets of those initiatives. These actions were used to affirm Portugal's status as a [transcontinental](/wiki/List_of_transcontinental_countries "List of transcontinental countries") nation and not a colonial empire.

Pro-Indian residents of [Dadra and Nagar Haveli](/wiki/Dadra_and_Nagar_Haveli "Dadra and Nagar Haveli"), separated those territories from Portuguese rule in 1954.[[90]](#cite_note-Lele-98) In 1961, [Fort of São João Baptista de Ajudá](/wiki/Fort_of_S%C3%A3o_Jo%C3%A3o_Baptista_de_Ajud%C3%A1 "Fort of São João Baptista de Ajudá")'s annexation by the [Republic of Dahomey](/wiki/Republic_of_Dahomey "Republic of Dahomey") was the start of a process that led to the dissolution of the centuries-old Portuguese Empire. Another forcible retreat occurred in 1961 when Portugal refused to relinquish [Goa](/wiki/Goa "Goa"). The Portuguese were involved in armed conflict in [Portuguese India](/wiki/Portuguese_India "Portuguese India") against the [Indian Armed Forces](/wiki/Indian_Armed_Forces "Indian Armed Forces"). The [operations resulted in the defeat](/wiki/Annexation_of_Goa "Annexation of Goa") and loss of the remaining Portuguese territories in the [Indian subcontinent](/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent"). The Portuguese regime refused to recognise Indian sovereignty over the annexed territories, which continued to be represented in the National Assembly until the coup of 1974.

Also in the early 1960s the independence movements in the Portuguese provinces of [Portuguese Angola](/wiki/Portuguese_Angola "Portuguese Angola"), [Portuguese Mozambique](/wiki/Portuguese_Mozambique "Portuguese Mozambique"), and [Portuguese Guinea](/wiki/Portuguese_Guinea "Portuguese Guinea") in Africa, resulted in the [Portuguese Colonial War](/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War "Portuguese Colonial War") (lasting from 1961 till 1974). The war mobilised around 1.4 million men for military or for civilian support service,[[91]](#cite_note-99) and led to large casualties. Throughout the colonial war period Portugal dealt with increasing dissent, arms embargoes and other punitive sanctions imposed by the international community. The authoritarian and conservative Estado Novo regime, tried to preserve the empire.[[92]](#cite_note-100)

Salazar governed until 1968, when he suffered a [brain hemorrhage](/wiki/Brain_hemorrhage "Brain hemorrhage"),[[93]](#cite_note-101) and was replaced by [Marcelo Caetano](/wiki/Marcelo_Caetano "Marcelo Caetano"), which raised hopes of a "liberalization" of the Estado Novo regime, called the *Marcelist Spring*, but those hopes were soon crushed.[[94]](#cite_note-102)

### Carnation Revolution and return to democracy

Main article: [Carnation Revolution](/wiki/Carnation_Revolution "Carnation Revolution")

The government and army resisted the [decolonization](/wiki/Decolonization "Decolonization") of its overseas territories until April 1974, when a left-wing military [coup](/wiki/Coup "Coup") in Lisbon, the Carnation Revolution, led the way for the independence of territories, as well as the restoration of democracy after two years of a transitional period known as PREC (*[Processo Revolucionário Em Curso](/wiki/Processo_Revolucion%C3%A1rio_Em_Curso "Processo Revolucionário Em Curso")*). This period was characterised by power disputes between left- and right-wing political forces. By the summer of 1975, the tensions were so high, that the country was on the verge of civil war. Forces connected to the extreme left-wing launched another coup on 25 November, but a military faction, the Group of Nine, initiated a counter-coup.

The Group of Nine emerged victorious, preventing the establishment of a [communist state](/wiki/Communist_state "Communist state") and ending political instability. The retreat from the overseas territories prompted a mass exodus of Portuguese citizens from its African territories.[[95]](#cite_note-103)[[96]](#cite_note-104) Over one million [Portuguese refugees](/wiki/White_African#Portuguese_in_Africa "White African") fled the former Portuguese provinces, as white settlers were usually not considered part of the former colonies. By 1975, all Portuguese African territories were independent and Portugal held [its first democratic elections](/wiki/1975_Portuguese_Constituent_Assembly_election "1975 Portuguese Constituent Assembly election") in 50 years.

Portugal continued to be governed by a [National Salvation Junta](/wiki/National_Salvation_Junta "National Salvation Junta") until the [Portuguese legislative election of 1976](/wiki/1976_Portuguese_legislative_election "1976 Portuguese legislative election"). It was won by the [Portuguese Socialist Party](/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Portugal) "Socialist Party (Portugal)") and Mário Soares, its leader, became prime minister. Soares would be prime minister from 1976 to 1978 and 1983 to 1985. Soares tried to resume the economic growth and development record that had been achieved before the Carnation Revolution. He initiated the process of accession to the [European Economic Community](/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community") (EEC).

After the transition to democracy, Portugal flipped between [socialism](/wiki/Socialism "Socialism") and adherence to the [neoliberal](/wiki/Neoliberal "Neoliberal") model. [Land reform](/wiki/Land_reform "Land reform") and [nationalisations](/wiki/Nationalization "Nationalization") were enforced; the [Portuguese Constitution](/wiki/Portuguese_Constitution "Portuguese Constitution") was rewritten to accommodate socialist and communist principles. Until the revisions of 1982 and 1989, the constitution had references to socialism, the rights of workers, and the desirability of a [socialist economy](/wiki/Socialist_economy "Socialist economy"). Portugal's economic situation after the revolution obliged the government to pursue [International Monetary Fund](/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund") (IMF)-monitored stabilisation programmes in 1977–78 and 1983–85.

In 1986 Portugal alongside Spain, joined the European Economic Community which later became the [European Union](/wiki/European_Union "European Union") (EU). Portugal's economy progressed considerably as a result of [European Structural and Investment Funds](/wiki/European_Structural_and_Investment_Funds "European Structural and Investment Funds") and companies' easier access to foreign markets.

Portugal's last overseas territory, [Macau](/wiki/Macau "Macau"), was peacefully handed over to China in 1999. In 2002, the independence of [East Timor](/wiki/East_Timor "East Timor") (Asia) was formally recognised by Portugal. In 1995, Portugal started to implement [Schengen Area](/wiki/Schengen_Area "Schengen Area") rules, eliminating border controls with other Schengen members. [Expo '98](/wiki/Expo_%2798 "Expo '98") took place in Portugal and in 1999 it was one of the founding countries of the [euro](/wiki/Euro "Euro") and [eurozone](/wiki/Eurozone "Eurozone"). In 2004 [José Manuel Barroso](/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_Barroso "José Manuel Barroso"), the then [Prime Minister of Portugal](/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal "Prime Minister of Portugal"), was nominated [President of the European Commission](/wiki/President_of_the_European_Commission "President of the European Commission"). On 1 December 2009 the [Treaty of Lisbon](/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon "Treaty of Lisbon") entered into force, enhancing the efficiency and democratic legitimacy of the Union. Economic disruption and an unsustainable growth in [government debt](/wiki/Government_debt "Government debt") during the [2008 financial crisis](/wiki/2008_financial_crisis "2008 financial crisis") led the country to negotiate in 2011 with the IMF and the European Union, through the [European Financial Stability Mechanism](/wiki/European_Financial_Stability_Mechanism "European Financial Stability Mechanism") and the [European Financial Stability Facility](/wiki/European_Financial_Stability_Facility "European Financial Stability Facility"), a loan to help the country stabilise its finances.

Geography
---------

Main article: [Geography of Portugal](/wiki/Geography_of_Portugal "Geography of Portugal")

Portugal occupies an area on the [Iberian Peninsula](/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") (referred to as *the continent* by most Portuguese) and two archipelagos in the Atlantic Ocean: [Madeira](/wiki/Madeira "Madeira") and the [Azores](/wiki/Azores "Azores"). It lies between latitudes [30°](/wiki/30th_parallel_north "30th parallel north") and [42° N](/wiki/42nd_parallel_north "42nd parallel north"), and longitudes [32°](/wiki/32nd_meridian_west "32nd meridian west") and [6° W](/wiki/6th_meridian_west "6th meridian west").

[Continental Portugal](/wiki/Continental_Portugal "Continental Portugal") is split by its main river, the [Tagus](/wiki/Tagus "Tagus"), that flows from Spain and disgorges in the [Tagus Estuary](/wiki/Tagus_Estuary_Natural_Reserve "Tagus Estuary Natural Reserve") at Lisbon, before escaping into the Atlantic. The northern landscape is mountainous towards the interior with several plateaus indented by river valleys, whereas the south, including the [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve") and the [Alentejo](/wiki/Alentejo "Alentejo") regions, is characterized by rolling plains.[[97]](#cite_note-105)

Portugal's highest peak is [Mount Pico](/wiki/Mount_Pico "Mount Pico") on [Pico Island](/wiki/Pico_Island "Pico Island") in the Azores. The archipelagos of Madeira and the Azores are scattered within the Atlantic Ocean: the Azores straddling the [Mid-Atlantic Ridge](/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_Ridge "Mid-Atlantic Ridge") on a tectonic triple junction, and Madeira along a range formed by [in-plate](/wiki/Plate_tectonics "Plate tectonics") [hotspot](/wiki/Hotspot_(geology) "Hotspot (geology)") geology. Geologically, these islands were formed by volcanic and seismic events. The last terrestrial volcanic eruption occurred in 1957–58 ([Capelinhos](/wiki/Capelinhos "Capelinhos")) and minor earthquakes occur sporadically.

[The exclusive economic zone](/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone_of_Portugal "Exclusive economic zone of Portugal"), a sea zone over which the Portuguese have special rights in exploration and have use of marine resources, covers an area of 1,727,408 km2 (666,956 sq mi). This is the 3rd largest [exclusive economic zone](/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone "Exclusive economic zone") of the European Union and the 20th largest in the world.[[98]](#cite_note-106)

The term "**provinces**" ([Portuguese](/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language"): *províncias*) has been used throughout history to identify regions of [continental Portugal](/wiki/Continental_Portugal "Continental Portugal"). Current legal [subdivisions of Portugal](/wiki/Subdivisions_of_Portugal "Subdivisions of Portugal") do not coincide with the provinces, but several provinces, in their 19th- and 20th-century versions, still correspond to culturally relevant, strongly self-identifying categories. They include:

* [Alentejo](/wiki/Alentejo "Alentejo") ([Alto Alentejo](/wiki/Alto_Alentejo_Province "Alto Alentejo Province"), [Baixo Alentejo](/wiki/Baixo_Alentejo_Province "Baixo Alentejo Province"))
* [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve")
* [Beira](/wiki/Beira_(Portugal) "Beira (Portugal)") ([Beira Alta](/wiki/Beira_Alta_Province "Beira Alta Province"), [Beira Baixa](/wiki/Beira_Baixa_Province "Beira Baixa Province"), [Beira Litoral](/wiki/Beira_Litoral_Province "Beira Litoral Province"))
* [Douro Litoral](/wiki/Douro_Litoral_Province "Douro Litoral Province")
* [Estremadura](/wiki/Estremadura_Province_(1936%E2%80%931976) "Estremadura Province (1936–1976)")
* [Minho](/wiki/Minho_Province "Minho Province")
* [Ribatejo](/wiki/Ribatejo_Province "Ribatejo Province")
* [Trás-os-Montes e Alto Douro](/wiki/Tr%C3%A1s-os-Montes_e_Alto_Douro_Province "Trás-os-Montes e Alto Douro Province")

The islands of [Azores](/wiki/Azores "Azores") and [Madeira](/wiki/Madeira "Madeira") were never called "provinces".

### Climate

Main article: [Climate of Portugal](/wiki/Climate_of_Portugal "Climate of Portugal")

Portugal is mainly characterised by a [Mediterranean climate](/wiki/Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate"),[[99]](#cite_note-107) [temperate maritime climate](/wiki/Oceanic_climate "Oceanic climate") in high altitude zones of the Azorean islands; a [semi-arid climate](/wiki/Semi-arid_climate "Semi-arid climate") in parts of the [Beja District](/wiki/Beja_District "Beja District") far south and in [Porto Santo Island](/wiki/Porto_Santo_Island "Porto Santo Island"), a [hot desert climate](/wiki/Hot_desert_climate "Hot desert climate") in the [Selvagens Islands](/wiki/Savage_Islands "Savage Islands") and a [humid subtropical climate](/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate "Humid subtropical climate") in the western [Azores](/wiki/Azores "Azores"), according to the [Köppen-Geiger climate classification](/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification"). It is one of the warmest countries in Europe: the average temperature in [mainland Portugal](/wiki/Mainland_Portugal "Mainland Portugal") varies from 10–12 °C (50.0–53.6 °F) in the mountainous interior north to 17–19 °C (62.6–66.2 °F) in the south and on the [Guadiana](/wiki/Guadiana "Guadiana") [river basin](/wiki/River_basin "River basin"). There are variations from the highlands to the lowlands.[[100]](#cite_note-108) The [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve"), separated from the [Alentejo](/wiki/Alentejo "Alentejo") region by mountains reaching up to 900 metres (3,000 ft) in [Alto da Fóia](/wiki/F%C3%B3ia_(mountain) "Fóia (mountain)"), has a climate similar to that of the southern coastal areas of Spain or Southwest Australia.

Annual average rainfall in the mainland varies from just over 3,200 millimetres (126.0 in) on the [Peneda-Gerês National Park](/wiki/Peneda-Ger%C3%AAs_National_Park "Peneda-Gerês National Park") to less than 500 millimetres (19.7 in) in southern parts of [Alentejo](/wiki/Alentejo "Alentejo"). [Mount Pico](/wiki/Mount_Pico "Mount Pico") receives the largest annual rainfall (over 6,250 millimetres (246.1 in) per year), according to *[Instituto Português do Mar e da Atmosfera](/wiki/Instituto_Portugu%C3%AAs_do_Mar_e_da_Atmosfera "Instituto Português do Mar e da Atmosfera")*. In some areas, such as the Guadiana basin, annual diurnal average temperatures can be as high as 24.5 °C (76.1 °F), and summer's highest temperatures are routinely over 40 °C (104 °F). The record high of 47.4 °C (117.3 °F) was recorded in [Amareleja](/wiki/Amareleja "Amareleja").[[101]](#cite_note-109)[[102]](#cite_note-110)

Snowfalls occur regularly during winter in the interior North and Centre, particularly in the highlands. In these regions, temperatures can drop below −10.0 °C (14.0 °F), and snow may fall any time from October to May. In the South, snowfalls are rare but can still occur at the highest elevations. While the official absolute minimum by [IPMA](/wiki/Instituto_Portugu%C3%AAs_do_Mar_e_da_Atmosfera "Instituto Português do Mar e da Atmosfera") is −16.0 °C (3.2 °F) in [Penhas da Saúde](/wiki/Penhas_da_Sa%C3%BAde "Penhas da Saúde") and [Miranda do Douro](/wiki/Miranda_do_Douro "Miranda do Douro"), lower temperatures have been recorded. Continental Portugal receives around 2,300-3,200 hours of sunshine annually, an average of 4–6 hours in winter and 10–12 hours in the summer, with higher values in the south-east, south-west, Algarve coast and lower in the north-west.

Portugal's central west and southwest coasts have an extreme ocean [seasonal lag](/wiki/Seasonal_lag "Seasonal lag"); sea temperatures are warmer in October than in July and are their coldest in March. The average [sea surface temperature](/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature "Sea surface temperature") on the west coast of mainland Portugal varies from 14–16 °C (57.2–60.8 °F) in January−March to 19–21 °C (66.2–69.8 °F) in August−October while on the south coast it ranges from 16 °C (60.8 °F) in January−March and rises in the summer to about 22–23 °C (71.6–73.4 °F), occasionally reaching 26 °C (78.8 °F).[[103]](#cite_note-111) In the Azores, around 16 °C (60.8 °F) in February−April to 22–24 °C (71.6–75.2 °F) in July−September,[[104]](#cite_note-112) and in Madeira, around 18 °C (64.4 °F) in February−April to 23–24 °C (73.4–75.2 °F) in August−October.[[105]](#cite_note-113)

Azores and Madeira have a subtropical climate, although variations between islands exist. The Madeira and Azorean archipelagos have a narrower temperature range, with annual average temperatures exceeding 20 °C (68 °F) in some parts of the coast.[[106]](#cite_note-114) Some islands in Azores have drier months in the summer. Consequently, the islands of the Azores have been identified as having a [Mediterranean climate](/wiki/Mediterranean_climate "Mediterranean climate"), while some islands (such as [Flores](/wiki/Flores_Island_(Azores) "Flores Island (Azores)") or [Corvo](/wiki/Corvo_Island "Corvo Island")) are classified as [Humid subtropical](/wiki/Humid_subtropical "Humid subtropical"), transitioning into an [Oceanic climate](/wiki/Oceanic_climate "Oceanic climate") at higher altitudes. [Porto Santo Island](/wiki/Porto_Santo_Island "Porto Santo Island") in Madeira has a warm semi-arid climate. The [Savage Islands](/wiki/Savage_Islands "Savage Islands"), which are part of the regional territory of Madeira and a nature reserve are unique in being classified as a desert climate with an annual average rainfall of approximately 150 millimetres (5.9 in).

[Climate change](/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") in Portugal is causing rising temperatures and longer-lasting [heat waves](/wiki/Heat_wave "Heat wave"), decreases in average rainfall and increases in the number of extremely rainy days (causing droughts and floods), and rising sea levels which will threaten the country's many coastal populations.[[107]](#cite_note-115)[[108]](#cite_note-IEA_2021-116) Wildfires are quite common and a major issue in Portugal,[[109]](#cite_note-117) being the country with the highest percentage of burned area, on average, in the entire [European Union](/wiki/European_Union "European Union").[[110]](#cite_note-118)[[111]](#cite_note-119)[[108]](#cite_note-IEA_2021-116)

### Biodiversity

Main article: [Biodiversity of Portugal](/wiki/Biodiversity_of_Portugal "Biodiversity of Portugal")

Portugal is located on the [Mediterranean Basin](/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin "Mediterranean Basin"), the third most diverse hotspot of flora in the world.[[112]](#cite_note-florestas-120) It is home to six terrestrial ecoregions: [Azores temperate mixed forests](/wiki/Azores_temperate_mixed_forests "Azores temperate mixed forests"), [Cantabrian mixed forests](/wiki/Cantabrian_mixed_forests "Cantabrian mixed forests"), [Madeira evergreen forests](/wiki/Madeira_evergreen_forests "Madeira evergreen forests"), [Iberian sclerophyllous and semi-deciduous forests](/wiki/Iberian_sclerophyllous_and_semi-deciduous_forests "Iberian sclerophyllous and semi-deciduous forests"), [Northwest Iberian montane forests](/wiki/Northwest_Iberian_montane_forests "Northwest Iberian montane forests"), and [Southwest Iberian Mediterranean sclerophyllous and mixed forests](/wiki/Southwest_Iberian_Mediterranean_sclerophyllous_and_mixed_forests "Southwest Iberian Mediterranean sclerophyllous and mixed forests").[[113]](#cite_note-DinersteinOlson2017-121) Over 22% of its land area is included in the [Natura 2000](/wiki/Natura_2000 "Natura 2000") network.[[114]](#cite_note-dre-122)[[112]](#cite_note-florestas-120) [Eucalyptus](/wiki/Eucalyptus "Eucalyptus"), [cork oak](/wiki/Cork_oak "Cork oak") and [maritime pine](/wiki/Maritime_pine "Maritime pine") together make up 71% of the total forested area of continental Portugal.[[115]](#cite_note-123)

Geographical and climatic conditions facilitate the introduction of exotic species that later turn to be invasive and destructive to the native habitats. Around 20 percent of the total number of extant species in continental Portugal are [exotic](/wiki/Introduced_species "Introduced species").[[116]](#cite_note-124) Portugal is the second country in Europe with the highest number of threatened animal and plant species.[[117]](#cite_note-125)[[118]](#cite_note-126) Portugal as a whole is an important stopover for migratory bird species.[[119]](#cite_note-127)[[120]](#cite_note-128)

The large mammalian species of Portugal (deer, [Iberian ibex](/wiki/Iberian_ibex "Iberian ibex"), [wild boar](/wiki/Wild_boar "Wild boar"), [red fox](/wiki/Red_fox "Red fox"), [Iberian wolf](/wiki/Iberian_wolf "Iberian wolf") and [Iberian lynx](/wiki/Iberian_lynx "Iberian lynx")) were once widespread throughout the country, but intense hunting, habitat degradation and growing pressure from agriculture and livestock reduced population on a large scale in the 19th and early 20th century, others, such as the [Portuguese ibex](/wiki/Portuguese_ibex "Portuguese ibex") were even led to extinction. Today, these animals are re-expanding their native range.[[121]](#cite_note-129)[[122]](#cite_note-mammals-130)

The Portuguese west coast is part of the four major Eastern Boundary [Upwelling](/wiki/Upwelling "Upwelling") Systems of the ocean. This seasonal upwelling system typically seen during the summer months brings cooler, nutrient rich water up to the sea surface promoting [phytoplankton](/wiki/Phytoplankton "Phytoplankton") growth, [zooplankton](/wiki/Zooplankton "Zooplankton") development and the subsequent rich diversity in [pelagic fish](/wiki/Pelagic_fish "Pelagic fish") and other [marine invertebrates](/wiki/Marine_invertebrates "Marine invertebrates").[[123]](#cite_note-131) This makes Portugal one of the largest per capita fish-consumers in the world.[[124]](#cite_note-132) 73% of the [freshwater fish](/wiki/Freshwater_fish "Freshwater fish") occurring in the [Iberian Peninsula](/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula") are endemic, the largest out of any region in Europe.[[125]](#cite_note-133) Some [protected areas of Portugal](/wiki/Protected_areas_of_Portugal "Protected areas of Portugal") include: the [Serras de Aire e Candeeiros](/wiki/Serras_de_Aire_e_Candeeiros_Natural_Park "Serras de Aire e Candeeiros Natural Park"),[[126]](#cite_note-134) the [Southwest Alentejo and Vicentine Coast Natural Park](/wiki/Southwest_Alentejo_and_Vicentine_Coast_Natural_Park "Southwest Alentejo and Vicentine Coast Natural Park"),[[127]](#cite_note-135) and the [Montesinho Natural Park](/wiki/Montesinho_Natural_Park "Montesinho Natural Park") which hosts some of the only populations of [Iberian wolf](/wiki/Iberian_wolf "Iberian wolf") and [Iberian brown bear](/wiki/Iberian_brown_bear "Iberian brown bear").[[128]](#cite_note-136)

Politics
--------

Main article: [Politics of Portugal](/wiki/Politics_of_Portugal "Politics of Portugal")

[Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa](/wiki/Marcelo_Rebelo_de_Sousa "Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa"),  
[President](/wiki/President_of_Portugal "President of Portugal") since 2016

[Luís Montenegro](/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Montenegro "Luís Montenegro"),  
[Prime Minister](/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal "Prime Minister of Portugal") since 2024

Portugal has been a [semi-presidential](/wiki/Semi-presidential_system "Semi-presidential system") representative democratic [republic](/wiki/Republic "Republic") since the ratification of the [Constitution of 1976](/wiki/Portuguese_Constitution_of_1976 "Portuguese Constitution of 1976"), with [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon"), the nation's largest city, as its capital.[[129]](#cite_note-137) The Constitution grants the division or separation of powers among four sovereignty bodies: the [President of the Republic](/wiki/President_of_Portugal "President of Portugal"), the [Assembly of the Republic](/wiki/Portuguese_parliament "Portuguese parliament"), the [Government](/wiki/Government_of_Portugal "Government of Portugal") and the [Courts](/wiki/Judiciary_of_Portugal "Judiciary of Portugal").[[130]](#cite_note-constitution-138)

The Head of State is the [President of the Republic](/wiki/President_of_Portugal "President of Portugal"), elected to a five-year term by direct, [universal suffrage](/wiki/Universal_suffrage "Universal suffrage"); the current president is [Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa](/wiki/Marcelo_Rebelo_de_Sousa "Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa"). Although largely a ceremonial post,[[131]](#cite_note-139) Presidential powers include the appointment of the Prime Minister and other members of the Government; dismissing the Prime Minister; dissolving the Assembly; [vetoing](/wiki/Veto "Veto") legislation (which may be overridden by the Assembly); and declaring war (only on the advice of the Government and with the authorisation of the Assembly).[[132]](#cite_note-140) The President has also supervisory and [reserve powers](/wiki/Reserve_power "Reserve power") and is the *[ex officio](/wiki/Ex_officio "Ex officio")* Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces. The President is advised on issues of importance by the [Council of State](/wiki/Portuguese_Council_of_State "Portuguese Council of State").[[133]](#cite_note-141)

### Government

Main article: [Government of Portugal](/wiki/Government_of_Portugal "Government of Portugal")

The [Assembly of the Republic](/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal) "Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)") is a single chamber parliament composed of a maximum of 230 deputies elected for a four-year term.[[134]](#cite_note-142) The Government is headed by the [Prime Minister](/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal "Prime Minister of Portugal") and includes Ministers and Secretaries of State, that have full executive powers;[[135]](#cite_note-143) the current prime minister is [Luís Montenegro](/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Montenegro "Luís Montenegro").[[136]](#cite_note-144) The [Council of Ministers](/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_(Portugal) "Council of Ministers (Portugal)") – under the Prime Minister (or the President at the latter's request) and the Ministers – acts as the [cabinet](/wiki/Cabinet_(government) "Cabinet (government)").[[137]](#cite_note-145) The [Courts](/wiki/Courts "Courts") are organized into several levels, among the judicial, administrative and fiscal branches. The [Supreme Courts](/wiki/Portuguese_Supreme_Court "Portuguese Supreme Court") are institutions of last resort/appeal. A thirteen-member [Constitutional Court](/wiki/Portuguese_Constitutional_Court "Portuguese Constitutional Court") oversees the constitutionality of the laws.[[138]](#cite_note-146)

Portugal operates a multi-party system of competitive legislatures/local administrative governments at the national, regional and local levels. The Assembly of the Republic, Regional Assemblies and local municipalities and parishes, are dominated by two political parties, the [Social Democratic Party](/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(Portugal) "Social Democratic Party (Portugal)") and the [Socialist Party](/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Portugal) "Socialist Party (Portugal)"), in addition to [Enough](/wiki/Chega_(political_party) "Chega (political party)"), the [Liberal Initiative](/wiki/Liberal_Initiative_(Portugal) "Liberal Initiative (Portugal)"), the [Left Bloc](/wiki/Left_Bloc_(Portugal) "Left Bloc (Portugal)"), the [Unitary Democratic Coalition](/wiki/Unitary_Democratic_Coalition "Unitary Democratic Coalition") ([Portuguese Communist Party](/wiki/Portuguese_Communist_Party "Portuguese Communist Party") and [Ecologist Party "The Greens"](/wiki/Ecologist_Party_%22The_Greens%22 "Ecologist Party \"The Greens\"")), [LIVRE](/wiki/LIVRE "LIVRE"), the [CDS – People's Party](/wiki/CDS_%E2%80%93_People%27s_Party "CDS – People's Party") and the [People Animals Nature](/wiki/People_Animals_Nature "People Animals Nature").[[139]](#cite_note-147)

### Foreign relations

Main article: [Foreign relations of Portugal](/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Portugal "Foreign relations of Portugal")

A member state of the [United Nations](/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") since 1955, Portugal is a founding member of [NATO](/wiki/NATO "NATO") (1949), the [OECD](/wiki/OECD "OECD") (1961) and [EFTA](/wiki/European_Free_Trade_Association "European Free Trade Association") (1960); it left the last in 1986 to join the [European Economic Community](/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community"), which became the European Union in 1993. In 1996, Portugal co-founded the [Community of Portuguese Language Countries](/wiki/Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries "Community of Portuguese Language Countries") (CPLP), also known as the Lusophone Commonwealth, an international organisation and political association of [Lusophone](/wiki/Lusophone "Lusophone") nations where [Portuguese](/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language") is an official language.

Portugal has hosted several international summits and events like the [first EU–Brazil summit](/wiki/1st_EU%E2%80%93Brazil_summit "1st EU–Brazil summit") in July 2007, the [second EU–African Union summit](/wiki/2nd_European_Union%E2%80%93African_Union_Summit "2nd European Union–African Union Summit") in December 2007, the signing of the [Treaty of Lisbon](/wiki/Signing_of_the_Treaty_of_Lisbon "Signing of the Treaty of Lisbon") also in December 2007, and the [NATO summit](/wiki/2010_Lisbon_summit "2010 Lisbon summit") in November 2010. Portugal was a full member of the [Latin Union](/wiki/Latin_Union "Latin Union") (1983) and the [Organisation of Ibero-American States](/wiki/Organization_of_Ibero-American_States "Organization of Ibero-American States") (1949). It has a friendship alliance and [dual citizenship](/wiki/Multiple_citizenship "Multiple citizenship") treaty with its former colony, Brazil. Portugal and the United Kingdom share the world's oldest active military accord through their [Anglo-Portuguese Alliance](/wiki/Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance "Anglo-Portuguese Alliance") ([Treaty of Windsor](/wiki/Treaty_of_Windsor_(1386) "Treaty of Windsor (1386)")), signed in 1373.

### Territorial disputes

[Olivenza](/wiki/Olivenza "Olivenza"): Under Portuguese sovereignty since 1297, the municipality of Olivença was ceded to Spain under the [Treaty of Badajoz](/wiki/Treaty_of_Badajoz_(1801) "Treaty of Badajoz (1801)") in 1801, after the [War of the Oranges](/wiki/War_of_the_Oranges "War of the Oranges"). Portugal claimed it back in 1815 under the [Treaty of Vienna](/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna "Congress of Vienna"). However, since the 19th century, it has been continuously ruled by Spain which considers the territory theirs not only *de facto* but also *de jure*.[[140]](#cite_note-One-europe.net_2017-09-29-148)

[Savage Islands](/wiki/Savage_Islands "Savage Islands"): A small group of mostly uninhabited islets which fall under Portuguese Madeira's regional autonomous jurisdiction. Found in 1364 by Italian mariners under the service of [Prince Henry The Navigator](/wiki/Prince_Henry_The_Navigator "Prince Henry The Navigator"),[[141]](#cite_note-149) it was first noted by Portuguese navigator Diogo Gomes de Sintra in 1438. Historically, the islands have belonged to private Portuguese owners from the 16th century on, until 1971[[142]](#cite_note-150) when the government purchased them and established a natural reserve area covering the whole archipelago. The islands have been claimed by Spain since 1911,[[143]](#cite_note-151) and the dispute has caused some periods of political tension between the two countries.[[144]](#cite_note-152) The main problem for Spain's attempts to claim these small islands, has been not so much their intrinsic value, but the fact that they expand Portugal's [exclusive economic zone](/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone "Exclusive economic zone") considerably to the south, in detriment of Spain.[[145]](#cite_note-Algarvedailynews.com_2017-09-29-153) The Selvagens Islands have been tentatively added to UNESCO's world heritage list in 2017.[[146]](#cite_note-154)

### Military

Main article: [Portuguese Armed Forces](/wiki/Portuguese_Armed_Forces "Portuguese Armed Forces")

The armed forces have three branches: [Navy](/wiki/Portuguese_Navy "Portuguese Navy"), [Army](/wiki/Portuguese_Army "Portuguese Army") and [Air Force](/wiki/Portuguese_Air_Force "Portuguese Air Force"), commanded by the [Portuguese Armed Forces General Staff](/wiki/Armed_Forces_General_Staff_(Portugal) "Armed Forces General Staff (Portugal)"). They serve primarily as a self-defence force whose mission is to protect the territorial integrity of the country but can also be used in offensive missions in foreign territories.[[147]](#cite_note-155) In recent years, the [Portuguese Armed Forces](/wiki/Portuguese_Armed_Forces "Portuguese Armed Forces") have carried out several [NATO](/wiki/NATO "NATO") and [European Union](/wiki/European_Union "European Union") military missions in various territories, namely in [Afghanistan](/wiki/Afghanistan "Afghanistan"), [Iraq](/wiki/Iraq "Iraq"), [Lebanon](/wiki/Lebanon "Lebanon"), [Bosnia and Herzegovina](/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina "Bosnia and Herzegovina"), [Kosovo](/wiki/Kosovo "Kosovo"), [Mali](/wiki/Mali "Mali"), [Central African Republic](/wiki/Central_African_Republic "Central African Republic"), [Somalia](/wiki/Somalia "Somalia"), [Mozambique](/wiki/Mozambique "Mozambique") and [East Timor](/wiki/East_Timor "East Timor"). As of 2023, the three branches numbered 24.000 military personnel. [Portuguese military expenditure](/wiki/List_of_countries_by_military_expenditures "List of countries by military expenditures") in 2023 was more than 4 billion US$, representing 1.48 per cent of GDP.[[148]](#cite_note-156)

The Army of 11,000 personnel comprises three brigades and other small units. An [Infantry Brigade](/wiki/Portuguese_Intervention_Brigade "Portuguese Intervention Brigade") (mainly equipped with [Pandur II](/wiki/Pandur_II "Pandur II") [APC](/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier "Armoured personnel carrier"), [M114 howitzer](/wiki/M114_155_mm_howitzer "M114 155 mm howitzer") and [MIM-72 Chaparral](/wiki/MIM-72_Chaparral "MIM-72 Chaparral") [air defence systems](/wiki/Air_defence "Air defence")), a [Mechanized Brigade](/wiki/Portuguese_Mechanized_Brigade "Portuguese Mechanized Brigade") (mainly equipped with [Leopard 2 A6](/wiki/Leopard_2 "Leopard 2") tanks and [M113A2](/wiki/M113 "M113") APC) and a [Rapid Reaction Brigade](/wiki/Portuguese_Rapid_Reaction_Brigade "Portuguese Rapid Reaction Brigade") (consisting of [Paratroopers](/wiki/Portuguese_Paratroopers "Portuguese Paratroopers"), [Commandos](/wiki/Portuguese_Army_Commandos "Portuguese Army Commandos"), [Rangers](/wiki/Special_Operations_Troops_Centre "Special Operations Troops Centre") and [Artillery Regiment](/wiki/4th_Artillery_Regiment_(Portugal) "4th Artillery Regiment (Portugal)")). The Navy (7,000 personnel, of which 900 are [marines](/wiki/Portuguese_Marines "Portuguese Marines")), the world's oldest surviving naval force, has five frigates, two corvettes, two submarines, and 20 oceanic patrol vessels. The Air Force (6,000 personnel) has the [Lockheed F-16M Fighting Falcon](/wiki/Lockheed_F-16_Fighting_Falcon "Lockheed F-16 Fighting Falcon") as the main combat aircraft.

In addition to the three branches of the armed forces, there is the [National Republican Guard](/wiki/National_Republican_Guard_(Portugal) "National Republican Guard (Portugal)"), a security force subject to military law and organisation ([gendarmerie](/wiki/Gendarmerie "Gendarmerie")) comprising 25,000 personnel. This force is under the authority of both the Defence and the Interior Ministry. It has provided detachments for participation in international operations in Iraq and East Timor. The United States maintains a military presence with 770 troops in the [Lajes Air Base](/wiki/Lajes_Air_Base "Lajes Air Base") at [Terceira Island](/wiki/Terceira_Island "Terceira Island"), in the Azores. The [Allied Joint Force Command Lisbon](/wiki/Allied_Joint_Force_Command_Lisbon "Allied Joint Force Command Lisbon") (JFC Lisbon) is one of the three main subdivisions of [NATO](/wiki/NATO "NATO")'s [Allied Command Operations](/wiki/Allied_Command_Operations "Allied Command Operations").

### Law and justice

Main article: [Judiciary of Portugal](/wiki/Judiciary_of_Portugal "Judiciary of Portugal")

The Portuguese legal system is part of the civil law legal system. The main laws include the Constitution (1976), the [Portuguese Civil Code](/wiki/Portuguese_Civil_Code "Portuguese Civil Code") (1966) and the [Penal Code of Portugal](/wiki/Penal_Code_of_Portugal "Penal Code of Portugal") (1982), as amended. Other relevant laws are the *Commercial Code* (1888) and the *Civil Procedure Code* (1961). Portuguese laws were applied in the former [colonies and territories](/wiki/Portuguese_colonies "Portuguese colonies") and continue to be influences for those countries. The supreme national courts are the [Supreme Court of Justice](/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Justice_(Portugal) "Supreme Court of Justice (Portugal)") and the [Constitutional Court](/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Portugal "Constitutional Court of Portugal"). The [Public Ministry](/wiki/Public_Prosecution_Service_(Portugal) "Public Prosecution Service (Portugal)"), headed by the Attorney General of the Republic, constitutes the independent body of public prosecutors.

Drug [decriminalisation](/wiki/Decriminalisation "Decriminalisation") was declared in 2001, making Portugal the first country to allow usage and personal possession of all common drugs. Despite criticism from other European nations, who stated Portugal's drug consumption would tremendously increase, overall drug use has declined along with [HIV](/wiki/HIV "HIV") infection cases, which dropped 50 percent by 2009. Overall drug use among 16- to 18-year-olds declined, however use of marijuana rose slightly.[[149]](#cite_note-157)[[150]](#cite_note-158)[[151]](#cite_note-159)

[LGBT rights in Portugal](/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Portugal "LGBT rights in Portugal") have increased substantially in the 21st century. In 2003, Portugal added an anti-discrimination employment law on the basis of sexual orientation.[[152]](#cite_note-160) In 2004, sexual orientation was added to the Constitution as part of the protected from discrimination characteristics.[[153]](#cite_note-161) In 2010, Portugal became the sixth country in Europe and eighth in the world to legalize [same-sex marriage](/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_Portugal "Same-sex marriage in Portugal") at the national level.[[154]](#cite_note-autogenerated2-162)

[LGBT adoption](/wiki/LGBT_adoption "LGBT adoption") has been allowed since 2016[[155]](#cite_note-163) as has female same-sex couple access to medically assisted reproduction.[[156]](#cite_note-164) In 2017 the *Law of Gender Identity*,[[157]](#cite_note-165) simplified the legal process of [gender](/wiki/Gender "Gender") and name change for [transgender](/wiki/Transgender "Transgender") people, making it easier for minors to change their [sex marker](/wiki/Sex_marker "Sex marker") in legal documents.[[158]](#cite_note-166) In 2018, the right to [gender identity](/wiki/Gender_identity "Gender identity") and [gender expression](/wiki/Gender_expression "Gender expression") self-determination became protected, [intersex](/wiki/Intersex "Intersex") minors became protected by law from unnecessary [medical procedures](/wiki/Intersex_medical_interventions "Intersex medical interventions") "until the minor gender identity manifests" and the right of protection from discrimination on the basis of [sex characteristics](/wiki/Sex_characteristics_(legal_term) "Sex characteristics (legal term)") became protected by the same law.[[159]](#cite_note-167)

Over the past 30 years, [Euthanasia](/wiki/Euthanasia "Euthanasia") legalization has been proposed and approved in several parliamentary reviews, but has been blocked by the [Constitutional Court](/wiki/Constitutional_Court_(Portugal) "Constitutional Court (Portugal)"), with the most recent unfavorable decision dating from 2025.[[160]](#cite_note-168) In the most recent proposal, national residents over 18 who were terminally ill and in extreme suffering, but who could still decide to, would have the legal right to request for assisted dying. For non-residents or foreigners euthanasia would not be allowed.[[161]](#cite_note-169)

### Law enforcement

Main article: [Law enforcement in Portugal](/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_Portugal "Law enforcement in Portugal")

Portugal's main police organisations are the *[Guarda Nacional Republicana](/wiki/Guarda_Nacional_Republicana "Guarda Nacional Republicana") – GNR* ([National Republican Guard](/wiki/National_Republican_Guard_(Portugal) "National Republican Guard (Portugal)")), a [gendarmerie](/wiki/Gendarmerie "Gendarmerie"); the *[Polícia de Segurança Pública](/wiki/Pol%C3%ADcia_de_Seguran%C3%A7a_P%C3%BAblica "Polícia de Segurança Pública") – PSP* (Public Security Police), a civilian police force who work in urban areas; and the *[Polícia Judiciária](/wiki/Pol%C3%ADcia_Judici%C3%A1ria "Polícia Judiciária") – PJ* (Judicial Police), a highly specialised criminal investigation police that is overseen by the Public Ministry.

Portugal has 49 correctional facilities in total run by the Ministry of Justice. They include seventeen central prisons, four special prisons, twenty-seven regional prisons, and one 'Cadeia de Apoio' (Support Detention Centre).[[162]](#cite_note-170) As of 1 January 2023[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Portugal&action=edit), their current prison population is about 12,257 inmates, which comes to about 0.12% of their entire population.[[163]](#cite_note-171) The incarceration rate has been on the rise since 2010, with a 15% increase over the past eight years.[[164]](#cite_note-prisons_2021-172)

### Administrative divisions

Main article: [Administrative divisions of Portugal](/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Portugal "Administrative divisions of Portugal")

Administratively, Portugal is divided into 308 [municipalities](/wiki/Municipalities_of_Portugal "Municipalities of Portugal") (*municípios* or *[concelhos](/wiki/Concelhos "Concelhos")*), which after a reform in [2013](/wiki/2013_Portuguese_local_election "2013 Portuguese local election") are subdivided into 3,092 civil parishes ([Portuguese](/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language"): *[freguesia](/wiki/Freguesia "Freguesia")*). Operationally, the municipality and civil parish, along with the national government, are the only legally [local administrative units](/wiki/Local_administrative_unit "Local administrative unit") identified by the government of Portugal (for example, cities, towns or villages have no standing in law, although may be used as catchment for the defining services). *Continental Portugal* is agglomerated into 18 districts, while the archipelagos of the Azores and Madeira are governed as [autonomous regions](/wiki/Autonomous_area "Autonomous area"); the largest units, established since 1976, are either mainland Portugal and the [autonomous regions of Portugal](/wiki/Autonomous_regions_of_Portugal "Autonomous regions of Portugal") ([Azores](/wiki/Azores "Azores") and [Madeira](/wiki/Madeira "Madeira")).

The 18 districts of mainland Portugal are: [Aveiro](/wiki/Aveiro_District "Aveiro District"), [Beja](/wiki/Beja_District "Beja District"), [Braga](/wiki/Braga_District "Braga District"), [Bragança](/wiki/Bragan%C3%A7a_District "Bragança District"), [Castelo Branco](/wiki/Castelo_Branco_District "Castelo Branco District"), [Coimbra](/wiki/Coimbra_District "Coimbra District"), [Évora](/wiki/%C3%89vora_District "Évora District"), [Faro](/wiki/Faro_District "Faro District"), [Guarda](/wiki/Guarda_District "Guarda District"), [Leiria](/wiki/Leiria_District "Leiria District"), [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon_District "Lisbon District"), [Portalegre](/wiki/Portalegre_District "Portalegre District"), [Porto](/wiki/Porto_District "Porto District"), [Santarém](/wiki/Santar%C3%A9m_District "Santarém District"), [Setúbal](/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal_District "Setúbal District"), [Viana do Castelo](/wiki/Viana_do_Castelo_District "Viana do Castelo District"), [Vila Real](/wiki/Vila_Real_District "Vila Real District") and [Viseu](/wiki/Viseu_District "Viseu District") – each district takes the name of the district capital.

Within the European Union NUTS system, Portugal is divided into nine regions:[[165]](#cite_note-Official_Journal_2023-173) the [Azores](/wiki/Azores "Azores"), [Alentejo](/wiki/Alentejo "Alentejo"), [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve"), [Centro](/wiki/Centro,_Portugal "Centro, Portugal"), [Lisboa](/wiki/Lisboa_Region "Lisboa Region"), [Madeira](/wiki/Madeira "Madeira"), [Norte](/wiki/Norte,_Portugal "Norte, Portugal"), [Oeste e Vale do Tejo](/wiki/Oeste_e_Vale_do_Tejo "Oeste e Vale do Tejo") and [Península de Setúbal](/wiki/Pen%C3%ADnsula_de_Set%C3%BAbal "Península de Setúbal"), and with the exception of the Azores and Madeira, NUTS areas are subdivided into 28 subregions. Economy
-------

Main article: [Economy of Portugal](/wiki/Economy_of_Portugal "Economy of Portugal")

Portugal is a [developed](/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country") and [high-income country](/wiki/High_income_countries "High income countries")[[168]](#cite_note-176)[[169]](#cite_note-177)[[170]](#cite_note-Quality-178) with a GDP per capita of 82% of the EU27 average in 2024,[[171]](#cite_note-179) and a [HDI](/wiki/List_of_countries_by_Human_Development_Index "List of countries by Human Development Index") of 0.874 (the 42nd highest in the world) in 2022.[[172]](#cite_note-180)[[173]](#cite_note-181) It holds the [13th largest gold reserve in the world](/wiki/Gold_reserve "Gold reserve") at its [national central bank](/wiki/Bank_of_Portugal "Bank of Portugal"),[[174]](#cite_note-182) has the 8th largest proven reserves of [lithium](/wiki/Lithium "Lithium"),[[175]](#cite_note-183)[[176]](#cite_note-184)[[177]](#cite_note-185) with total exports representing 46.6% of its GDP in 2024.[[178]](#cite_note-186) Portugal has been a net beneficiary of the [European Union budget](/wiki/Budget_of_the_European_Union "Budget of the European Union") since it joined the union, then known as [EEC](/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community"), in 1986.[[179]](#cite_note-187)[[180]](#cite_note-188)[[181]](#cite_note-189)[[182]](#cite_note-190)

By the end of 2023, GDP (PPP) was $47,331 per capita, according to the [World Bank](/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank").[[183]](#cite_note-191) In 2023, Portugal had the 5th lowest GDP per capita (PPP) of the eurozone out of 20 members, and the 8th lowest of the European Union out of 27 member-states.[[184]](#cite_note-192) In 2022, [labour productivity](/wiki/Workforce_productivity "Workforce productivity") had fallen to the fourth lowest among the 27 member-states of the European Union (EU) and was 35% lower than the EU average.[[185]](#cite_note-193)

Portugal was an original member of the [eurozone](/wiki/Eurozone "Eurozone"). The national currency, the [euro](/wiki/Euro "Euro") (€) started transitioning from the [Portuguese Escudo](/wiki/Portuguese_Escudo "Portuguese Escudo") in 2000 and consolidated in 2002. Portugal's central bank is the *[Banco de Portugal](/wiki/Banco_de_Portugal "Banco de Portugal")*, an integral part of the [European System of Central Banks](/wiki/European_System_of_Central_Banks "European System of Central Banks"). Most industries, businesses and financial institutions are concentrated in the [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon_metropolitan_area "Lisbon metropolitan area") and [Porto](/wiki/Greater_Metropolitan_Area_of_Porto "Greater Metropolitan Area of Porto") metropolitan areas – the [Setúbal](/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal_District "Setúbal District"), [Aveiro](/wiki/Aveiro_District "Aveiro District"), [Braga](/wiki/Braga_District "Braga District"), [Coimbra](/wiki/Coimbra_District "Coimbra District"), [Leiria](/wiki/Leiria_District "Leiria District") and [Faro](/wiki/Faro_District "Faro District") districts are the biggest economic centres outside these two main areas.

Since the [Carnation Revolution](/wiki/Carnation_Revolution "Carnation Revolution") of 1974, which culminated in the end of one of Portugal's most notable [phases of economic expansion](/wiki/Economic_history_of_Portugal "Economic history of Portugal"),[[186]](#cite_note-194) a significant change has occurred in the nation's annual economic growth.[[187]](#cite_note-195) After the turmoil of the 1974 revolution, Portugal tried to adapt to a changing [modern global economy](/wiki/Globalization "Globalization"), a process that continues. Since the 1990s, Portugal's [public consumption](/wiki/Consumption_(economics) "Consumption (economics)")-based [economic development](/wiki/Economic_development "Economic development") model has changed to a system focused on exports, private [investment](/wiki/Investment_(macroeconomics) "Investment (macroeconomics)") and the development of the [high-tech](/wiki/High-tech "High-tech") sector. Consequently, business services have overtaken more traditional industries such as textiles, clothing, footwear and [cork](/wiki/Cork_(material) "Cork (material)") (Portugal is the world's leading cork producer),[[188]](#cite_note-196) wood products and beverages.[[189]](#cite_note-autogenerated1-197)

In the 2010s, the Portuguese economy suffered its most severe recession since the 1970s, which resulted in the country receiving a 78-billion-euro bailout from the [European Union](/wiki/European_Union "European Union") and the [International Monetary Fund](/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund") in May 2011.[[190]](#cite_note-nytimes-198) By end of 2023, the share of debt as percentage of GDP fell below 100 percent, to 97.9%,[[191]](#cite_note-199) and fell further to 94.9% by the end of 2024.[[192]](#cite_note-200)

In 2024, the average gross salary was €1,602 per month,[[193]](#cite_note-201) and the [minimum wage](/wiki/Minimum_wage "Minimum wage"), which is regulated by law, is €870 per month (paid 14 times per annum) as of 2025.[[194]](#cite_note-202) The [Global Competitiveness Report](/wiki/Global_Competitiveness_Report "Global Competitiveness Report") for 2019, published by the [World Economic Forum](/wiki/World_Economic_Forum "World Economic Forum"), placed Portugal 34th. The Numbeo quality of life index placed Portugal 20th in the world in 2023.[[170]](#cite_note-Quality-178)

Companies listed on [Euronext Lisbon](/wiki/Euronext_Lisbon "Euronext Lisbon") [stock exchange](/wiki/Stock_exchange "Stock exchange") like [EDP](/wiki/Energias_de_Portugal "Energias de Portugal"), [Galp](/wiki/Galp_Energia "Galp Energia"), [Jerónimo Martins](/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_Martins "Jerónimo Martins"), [Mota-Engil](/wiki/Mota-Engil "Mota-Engil"), Novabase, [Semapa](/wiki/Semapa "Semapa"), [Portucel Soporcel](/wiki/Portucel_Soporcel "Portucel Soporcel"), [Portugal Telecom](/wiki/Portugal_Telecom "Portugal Telecom") and [Sonae](/wiki/Sonae "Sonae"), are among the largest corporations by number of employees, [net income](/wiki/Net_income "Net income") or international [market share](/wiki/Market_share "Market share"). The Euronext Lisbon is the major stock exchange and part of the pan-European group of stock exchanges [Euronext](/wiki/Euronext "Euronext"). The [PSI-20](/wiki/PSI-20 "PSI-20") is Portugal's most selective and widely known [stock index](/wiki/Stock_index "Stock index").

The [OECD](/wiki/OECD "OECD") economic reports since 2018 show recovery.[[195]](#cite_note-203)[[196]](#cite_note-204)[[197]](#cite_note-205) Rents and house prices have skyrocketed in Portugal, particularly Lisbon, where rents jumped 37% in 2022. The 8% inflation rate in the same year exacerbated the problem.[[198]](#cite_note-206) According to the IMF, Portugal's economic recovery from the COVID pandemic in 2022 was substantially better than the EU average. Although modest, economic growth continued in 2023 while inflation continued decreasing to 5%.[[199]](#cite_note-207)[[200]](#cite_note-208) In 2024 the annual inflation continued a downward trend ending at 2.3% and accompanied by a small economic growth.[[201]](#cite_note-209)[[202]](#cite_note-210) In 2025, the economy is expected to continue growing at 1.9 annually, while inflation is forecast at 2.1% for the fiscal year. These moderately optimistic indicators are supported by increased private consumption, investment, employment growth and unemployment easing.[[203]](#cite_note-211)

Agriculture in Portugal is based on small to medium-sized family-owned dispersed units. However, the sector also includes larger scale [intensive farming](/wiki/Intensive_farming "Intensive farming"), export-oriented [agrobusinesses](/wiki/Agrobusiness "Agrobusiness"). The country produces a variety of crops and livestock products, including: tomatoes, [citrus](/wiki/Citrus "Citrus"), [green vegetables](/wiki/Green_vegetables "Green vegetables"), [rice](/wiki/Rice "Rice"), [wheat](/wiki/Wheat "Wheat"), [barley](/wiki/Barley "Barley"), [maize](/wiki/Maize "Maize"), [olives](/wiki/Olive "Olive"), [oilseeds](/wiki/Oilseed "Oilseed"), [nuts](/wiki/Nut_(fruit) "Nut (fruit)"), [cherries](/wiki/Cherry "Cherry"), [bilberry](/wiki/Bilberry "Bilberry"), [table grapes](/wiki/Table_grapes "Table grapes"), [edible mushrooms](/wiki/Edible_mushroom "Edible mushroom"), [dairy products](/wiki/Dairy_products "Dairy products"), [poultry](/wiki/Poultry "Poultry") and [beef](/wiki/Beef "Beef"). According to [FAO](/wiki/FAO "FAO"), Portugal is the top producer of [cork](/wiki/Cork_(material) "Cork (material)") and [carob](/wiki/Carob "Carob") in the world, accounting for about 50% and 30% of world production, respectively.[[204]](#cite_note-212) It is the third largest exporter of [chestnuts](/wiki/Chestnut "Chestnut") and third largest European producer of [pulp](/wiki/Pulp_(paper) "Pulp (paper)").[[205]](#cite_note-213) Portugal is among the world's top ten largest [olive oil](/wiki/Olive_oil "Olive oil") producers and fourth largest exporter.[[206]](#cite_note-214) The country is one of the world's largest exporters of [wine](/wiki/Wine "Wine"), reputed for its fine wines. [Forestry](/wiki/Forestry "Forestry") has played an important economic role among the rural communities and industry. In 2001, the gross agricultural product accounted for 4% of the economy; in 2022 it was 2%.[[207]](#cite_note-215)

### Tourism

Main article: [Tourism in Portugal](/wiki/Tourism_in_Portugal "Tourism in Portugal")

Travel and [tourism](/wiki/Tourism_in_Portugal "Tourism in Portugal") is an important part of Portugal's economy. As of 2023, nearly half of real GDP growth was due to the tourism sector, with tourism accounting for 16.5% of GDP.[[208]](#cite_note-216) It has been necessary for the country to focus upon its niche attractions, such as health, nature and rural tourism, to stay ahead of its competitors.[[209]](#cite_note-217)

Portugal is among the top 20 most-visited countries in the world, receiving more than 26,5 million foreign tourists by 2023.[[210]](#cite_note-218) In 2014, Portugal was elected *The Best European Country* by *[USA Today](/wiki/USA_Today "USA Today")*.[[211]](#cite_note-219) In 2017, Portugal was elected both *Europe's Leading Destination*[[212]](#cite_note-220) and in 2018 and 2019, *World's Leading Destination*[[213]](#cite_note-221)

Tourist hotspots in Portugal are: [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon"), [Cascais](/wiki/Cascais "Cascais"), [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve"), [Madeira](/wiki/Madeira "Madeira"), [Nazaré](/wiki/Nazar%C3%A9,_Portugal "Nazaré, Portugal"), [Fátima](/wiki/F%C3%A1tima,_Portugal "Fátima, Portugal"), [Óbidos](/wiki/%C3%93bidos,_Portugal "Óbidos, Portugal"), [Porto](/wiki/Porto "Porto"), [Braga](/wiki/Braga "Braga"), [Guimarães](/wiki/Guimar%C3%A3es "Guimarães") and [Coimbra](/wiki/Coimbra "Coimbra"). Lisbon attracts the sixteenth-most tourists among European cities[[214]](#cite_note-222) (with seven million tourists occupying the city's hotels in 2006).[[215]](#cite_note-223)

### Science and technology

Main article: [Science and technology in Portugal](/wiki/Science_and_technology_in_Portugal "Science and technology in Portugal")

Scientific and technological research activities are mainly conducted within a network of [R&D](/wiki/R%26D "R&D") units belonging to [public universities](/wiki/List_of_universities_in_Portugal "List of universities in Portugal") and state-managed autonomous research institutions like the [INETI – Instituto Nacional de Engenharia, Tecnologia e Inovação](/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_de_Engenharia,_Tecnologia_e_Inova%C3%A7%C3%A3o "Instituto Nacional de Engenharia, Tecnologia e Inovação") and the [INRB – Instituto Nacional dos Recursos Biológicos](/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_dos_Recursos_Biol%C3%B3gicos "Instituto Nacional dos Recursos Biológicos"). Funding and management of this system is conducted under the authority of the [Ministry of Science, Technology and Higher Education](/wiki/Ministry_of_Science,_Technology_and_Higher_Education "Ministry of Science, Technology and Higher Education") and the [Fundação para a Ciência e Tecnologia](/wiki/Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_para_a_Ci%C3%AAncia_e_Tecnologia "Fundação para a Ciência e Tecnologia") (Foundation for Science and Technology). The largest R&D units of the public universities by volume of research grants and peer-reviewed publications, include [biosciences](/wiki/Biosciences "Biosciences") research institutions.

Among the largest non-state-run research institutions are the [Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência](/wiki/Instituto_Gulbenkian_de_Ci%C3%AAncia "Instituto Gulbenkian de Ciência") and the [Champalimaud Foundation](/wiki/Champalimaud_Foundation "Champalimaud Foundation"), a neuroscience and oncology research centre. National and multinational high-tech and industrial companies, are responsible for research and development projects. One of the oldest learned societies of Portugal is the [Lisbon Academy of Sciences](/wiki/Lisbon_Academy_of_Sciences "Lisbon Academy of Sciences"), founded in 1779.

[Iberian](/wiki/Iberian_peninsula "Iberian peninsula") bilateral state-supported research efforts include the [International Iberian Nanotechnology Laboratory](/wiki/International_Iberian_Nanotechnology_Laboratory "International Iberian Nanotechnology Laboratory") and the [Ibercivis](/wiki/Ibercivis "Ibercivis") [distributed computing](/wiki/Distributed_computing "Distributed computing") platform. Portugal is a member of pan-European scientific organizations. These include the [European Space Agency](/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") (ESA), the [European Laboratory for Particle Physics](/wiki/European_Laboratory_for_Particle_Physics "European Laboratory for Particle Physics") (CERN), [ITER](/wiki/ITER "ITER"), and the [European Southern Observatory](/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory") (ESO). Portugal has the largest [aquarium](/wiki/Aquarium "Aquarium") in Europe, the [Lisbon Oceanarium](/wiki/Lisbon_Oceanarium "Lisbon Oceanarium"), and have other notable organizations focused on science-related exhibits and divulgation, like the state agency *Ciência Viva*,[[216]](#cite_note-224) the [Science Museum of the University of Coimbra](/wiki/Science_Museum_of_the_University_of_Coimbra "Science Museum of the University of Coimbra"), the [National Museum of Natural History](/wiki/List_of_natural_history_museums#Portugal "List of natural history museums") at the University of Lisbon, and the [Visionarium](/wiki/Visionarium_(Portugal) "Visionarium (Portugal)"). The [European Innovation Scoreboard](/wiki/European_Innovation_Scoreboard "European Innovation Scoreboard") 2011, placed Portugal-based innovation 15th, with increase in innovation expenditure and output.[[217]](#cite_note-225) Portugal was ranked 31st in the [Global Innovation Index](/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index "Global Innovation Index") in 2024.[[218]](#cite_note-226)

### Transport

Main article: [Transport in Portugal](/wiki/Transport_in_Portugal "Transport in Portugal")

Portugal has a 68,732 km (42,708 mi) road network, of which almost 3,000 km (1,864 mi) are part of system of 44 motorways. On many highways, a toll needs to be paid (see [Via Verde](/wiki/Via_Verde "Via Verde")). [Vasco da Gama bridge](/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama_bridge "Vasco da Gama bridge") is the longest bridge in the [EU](/wiki/European_Union "European Union") (the second longest in Europe) at 12.345 km (7.671 mi).[[220]](#cite_note-228)[[221]](#cite_note-229)

[Continental Portugal](/wiki/Continental_Portugal "Continental Portugal")'s 89,015 km2 (34,369 sq mi) territory is serviced by four international airports located near the principal cities of [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon"), [Porto](/wiki/Porto "Porto"), [Faro](/wiki/Faro,_Portugal "Faro, Portugal") and [Beja](/wiki/Beja_(Portugal) "Beja (Portugal)"). Lisbon's geographical position makes it a stopover for many foreign airlines at several airports within the country. The primary [flag-carrier](/wiki/Flag-carrier "Flag-carrier") is [TAP Air Portugal](/wiki/TAP_Air_Portugal "TAP Air Portugal"), although many other domestic airlines provide services within and without the country.

The most important airports are in [Lisbon](/wiki/Portela_Airport "Portela Airport"), [Porto](/wiki/Francisco_S%C3%A1_Carneiro_Airport "Francisco Sá Carneiro Airport"), [Faro](/wiki/Faro_Airport "Faro Airport"), [Funchal](/wiki/Madeira_Airport "Madeira Airport") (Madeira), and [Ponta Delgada](/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Paulo_II_Airport "João Paulo II Airport") (Azores), managed by the national airport authority group [ANA – Aeroportos de Portugal](/wiki/ANA_%E2%80%93_Aeroportos_de_Portugal "ANA – Aeroportos de Portugal"). A [new airport](/wiki/New_Lisbon_Airport "New Lisbon Airport"), to replace the current Lisbon airport, has been planned for more than 50 years, but it has been always postponed by a series of reasons.[[222]](#cite_note-230)

A national railway system that extends throughout the country and into Spain, is supported and administered by [Comboios de Portugal](/wiki/Comboios_de_Portugal "Comboios de Portugal") (CP). [Rail transport](/wiki/Rail_transport "Rail transport") of passengers and goods is derived using the 2,791 km (1,734 mi) of railway lines currently in service, of which 1,430 km (889 mi) are electrified and about 900 km (559 mi) allow train speeds greater than 120 km/h (75 mph). The railway network is managed by [Infraestruturas de Portugal](/wiki/Infraestruturas_de_Portugal "Infraestruturas de Portugal") while the transport of passengers and goods are the responsibility of CP, both public companies. In 2006, the CP carried 133,000,000 passengers and 9,750,000 [tonnes](/wiki/Tonne "Tonne") (9,600,000 [long tons](/wiki/Long_ton "Long ton"); 10,700,000 [short tons](/wiki/Short_ton "Short ton")) of goods.

The major seaports are located in [Sines](/wiki/Port_of_Sines "Port of Sines"), [Leixões](/wiki/Leix%C3%B5es "Leixões"), [Lisbon](/wiki/Port_of_Lisbon "Port of Lisbon"), [Setúbal](/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal "Setúbal"), [Aveiro](/wiki/Aveiro,_Portugal "Aveiro, Portugal"), [Figueira da Foz](/wiki/Figueira_da_Foz "Figueira da Foz"), and [Faro](/wiki/Faro,_Portugal "Faro, Portugal"). The two largest metropolitan areas have subway systems: [Lisbon Metro](/wiki/Lisbon_Metro "Lisbon Metro") and [Metro Sul do Tejo](/wiki/Metro_Transportes_do_Sul "Metro Transportes do Sul") light rail system in the [Lisbon metropolitan area](/wiki/Lisbon_metropolitan_area "Lisbon metropolitan area"), and [Porto Metro](/wiki/Porto_Metro "Porto Metro") [light metro](/wiki/Medium-capacity_rail_system "Medium-capacity rail system") system in the [Porto Metropolitan Area](/wiki/Porto_Metropolitan_Area "Porto Metropolitan Area"), each with more than 35 km (22 mi) of lines. [Coimbra](/wiki/Coimbra "Coimbra") is currently developing a [Bus rapid transit](/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit "Bus rapid transit") system, [Metro Mondego](/wiki/Metro_Mondego "Metro Mondego"), as is the [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve"), [Algarve Metrobus](/wiki/Algarve_Metrobus "Algarve Metrobus").

In Portugal, [Lisbon tram services](/wiki/Trams_in_Lisbon "Trams in Lisbon") have been supplied by the *Companhia de Carris de Ferro de Lisboa* ([Carris](/wiki/Carris "Carris")), for over a century. In [Porto](/wiki/Porto "Porto"), [a tram network](/wiki/Trams_in_Porto "Trams in Porto"), of which only a tourist line on the shores of the [Douro](/wiki/Douro "Douro") remains, began construction on 12 September 1895 (a first for the [Iberian Peninsula](/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula "Iberian Peninsula")). All major cities and towns have their own local urban transport network, as well as taxi services.

### Energy

Main article: [Energy in Portugal](/wiki/Energy_in_Portugal "Energy in Portugal")

As of 2023, oil made up 44% of Portugal's total energy supply. However the country phased out coal-fired generation in 2021 and has been developing [renewable energies](/wiki/Renewable_energy "Renewable energy") such as [hydopower](/wiki/Hydroelectricity "Hydroelectricity") and [wind power](/wiki/Wind_power "Wind power")[[223]](#cite_note-231) and investing in public transport and [electric vehicles](/wiki/Electric_vehicle "Electric vehicle").[[224]](#cite_note-232)[[225]](#cite_note-233)

Portugal has considerable wind and hydropower resources. In 2006, the world's then largest [solar power](/wiki/Solar_energy "Solar energy") plant, the [Moura Photovoltaic Power Station](/wiki/Moura_Photovoltaic_Power_Station "Moura Photovoltaic Power Station"), began operating, while the world's first commercial [wave power](/wiki/Wave_power "Wave power") farm, the [Aguçadoura Wave Farm](/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm "Aguçadoura Wave Farm"), opened in the [Norte region](/wiki/North_Region,_Portugal "North Region, Portugal") (2008). By 2006, 66% of the country's electrical production was from coal and fuel power plants, while 29% were derived from [hydroelectric](/wiki/Hydroelectric "Hydroelectric") dams, and 6% by [wind energy](/wiki/Wind_energy "Wind energy").[[226]](#cite_note-234) In 2008, renewable energy resources were producing 43% of the nation's electricity, even as hydroelectric production decreased with severe droughts.[[227]](#cite_note-235) As of 2010, electricity exports had outnumbered imports and 70% of energy came from renewable sources.[[228]](#cite_note-236)

Portugal's national energy transmission company, [Redes Energéticas Nacionais](/wiki/Redes_Energ%C3%A9ticas_Nacionais "Redes Energéticas Nacionais") (REN), uses modelling to predict weather, especially wind patterns. Before the solar/wind revolution, Portugal had generated electricity from hydropower plants on its rivers for decades. New programmes combine wind and water: wind-driven turbines pump water uphill at night; then water flows downhill by day, generating electricity, when consumer demand is highest. Portugal's distribution system is now two-way. It draws electricity small generators, like rooftop solar panels.

In 2023 Portugal emitted around 339 million tonnes of [greenhouse gases](/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas") (about 5 tonnes per person), equivalent to around 1% of global total emissions.[[229]](#cite_note-237) As an EU member state, Portugal is part of their joint plan to reduce emissions by a minimum of 55% by 2030, compared to the level of emissions in 1990. Portugal has committed to carbon neutrality and [net zero](/wiki/Net-zero_emissions "Net-zero emissions") by 2050.[[230]](#cite_note-238)

Demographics
------------

Main articles: [Demographics of Portugal](/wiki/Demographics_of_Portugal "Demographics of Portugal") and [Portuguese people](/wiki/Portuguese_people "Portuguese people")

Statistics Portugal ([Portuguese](/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language"): *INE – [Instituto Nacional de Estatística](/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_de_Estat%C3%ADstica_(Portugal) "Instituto Nacional de Estatística (Portugal)")*) estimates that, by 31 December 2023, the population was 10,639,726, of which 52.2% was female and 47.8% male.[[10]](#cite_note-Stat_Portugal-14)[[231]](#cite_note-INEPop-239) In 2024 the median life expectancy was 82.8 years[[232]](#cite_note-240) and United Nations projections point to 90 or above, by 2100.[[233]](#cite_note-241) The population has been relatively homogeneous for most of its history, with a single religion ([Catholic church](/wiki/Catholic_church "Catholic church")) and language.

Despite good economic development, the Portuguese have been the shortest in Europe since around 1890. This emerging height gap started in the 1840s and increased. A driving factor was modest real wage growth, given late industrialization and economic growth compared to the European core. Another determinant was delayed [human capital](/wiki/Human_capital "Human capital") formation.[[234]](#cite_note-242)

Portugal has to deal with low fertility levels: the country has experienced a [sub-replacement fertility rate](/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility_rate "Sub-replacement fertility rate") since the 1980s.[[235]](#cite_note-243) The total fertility rate (TFR) as of 2024[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Portugal&action=edit) was estimated at 1.36 children born/woman, one of the lowest in the world, similarly to countries such as Japan, South Korea, Italy, all well below the replacement rate of 2.1,[[236]](#cite_note-244)[[237]](#cite_note-245) and considerably below the high of 5 children born per woman in 1911.[[238]](#cite_note-246) In 2016, 53% of births were to unmarried women.[[239]](#cite_note-247) Portugal's population has been steadily ageing and was the 11th oldest in the world, with a median age of 46.4 years in 2024. In the same year, it had the world's 4th highest number of citizens over 65 years, at 21.8% of the whole population.[[15]](#cite_note-CIA-22)[[240]](#cite_note-248)

The structure of Portuguese society shows social inequality, which in 2019 placed the country 24th in the [Social Justice Index](/wiki/Social_justice_index "Social justice index"), in the EU.[[241]](#cite_note-249) In 2018, Portugal's parliament approved a budget plan for 2019 that included tax breaks for returning emigrants in a bid to attract back those who left during the [2008 financial crisis](/wiki/2008_financial_crisis "2008 financial crisis").[[242]](#cite_note-250) According to projections by the national statistics office, Portugal's population will fall to 7.7 million by 2080 from 10.6 million and the population will continue to age.[[243]](#cite_note-251)

According to a National Statistics Institute (INE) study, conducted shortly after the 2021 census, between 2022 and 2023, 6,4 million people aged between 18 and 74 years old identified themselves as White (84%), more than 262,000 identify as Mixed-race (3%), nearly 170,000 as Black (2%), 57,000 as Asian (<1%), and 47,500 as [Romani](/wiki/Romani_people_in_Portugal "Romani people in Portugal") (<1%)[[244]](#cite_note-INERace-252)

### Urbanization

Based on commuting patterns, OECD and Eurostat define eight [metropolitan areas of Portugal](/wiki/Metropolitan_areas_of_Portugal "Metropolitan areas of Portugal").[[245]](#cite_note-253) Only two have populations over 1 million, and since the 2013 local government reform, these are the only two which also have administrative legal status of metropolitan areas: [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon_metropolitan_area "Lisbon metropolitan area") and [Porto](/wiki/Greater_Metropolitan_Area_of_Porto "Greater Metropolitan Area of Porto"),[[246]](#cite_note-law75-254)[[247]](#cite_note-Oliveira-255) Several smaller metropolitan areas ([Algarve](/wiki/Greater_Metropolitan_Area_of_the_Algarve "Greater Metropolitan Area of the Algarve"), [Aveiro](/wiki/Greater_Metropolitan_Area_of_Aveiro "Greater Metropolitan Area of Aveiro"), [Coimbra](/wiki/Greater_Metropolitan_Area_of_Coimbra "Greater Metropolitan Area of Coimbra"), [Minho](/wiki/Greater_Metropolitan_Area_of_Minho "Greater Metropolitan Area of Minho") and [Viseu](/wiki/Greater_Metropolitan_Area_of_Viseu "Greater Metropolitan Area of Viseu"))[[247]](#cite_note-Oliveira-255) also held this status from 2003 to 2008, when they were converted into [intermunicipal communities](/wiki/Intermunicipal_communities_of_Portugal "Intermunicipal communities of Portugal"), whose territories are roughly based on the [NUTS III](/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics "Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics") statistical regions.[[248]](#cite_note-law45-256)[[247]](#cite_note-Oliveira-255)

### Immigration

Main article: [Immigration to Portugal](/wiki/Immigration_to_Portugal "Immigration to Portugal")

In 2023, Portugal had 10,639,726 inhabitants, of whom 1,293,463 accounted for legal resident foreigners.[[3]](#cite_note-aima-4)[[251]](#cite_note-259) Resident foreigners make up approximately 12% of the population. These figures do not include Portuguese citizens of foreign descent, as in Portugal it is illegal to collect data based on ethnicity. For instance, more than 340,000 resident foreigners who acquired [Portuguese citizenship](/wiki/Portuguese_nationality_law "Portuguese nationality law") between 2008 and 2022 - and thus constitute around 3.27% of the country's population in 2022 - were not taken into account in immigration figures as they became official Portuguese citizens.[[252]](#cite_note-260) In 2022 alone, almost 21,000 foreign residents acquired [Portuguese citizenship](/wiki/Portuguese_passport "Portuguese passport"), of which 11,170 were female and 9,674 were male.[[253]](#cite_note-261)

Portugal, for long a country of emigration (the vast majority of [Brazilians](/wiki/Portuguese_Brazilian "Portuguese Brazilian") have Portuguese ancestry),[[254]](#cite_note-diaspora-262) became a country of net immigration.[[255]](#cite_note-263) The influx of immigrants didn't come just from the last [Indian](/wiki/Portuguese_India "Portuguese India") (Portuguese until 1961), [African](/wiki/Portuguese-speaking_African_countries "Portuguese-speaking African countries") (Portuguese until 1975), and [Far East Asian](/wiki/Macau "Macau") (Portuguese until 1999) overseas territories, but from other parts of the world as well. Even though in the aftermath of the [COVID-19 Pandemic](/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Portugal "COVID-19 pandemic in Portugal"), Portugal's emigration rate increased to 6.9‰ in 2022, it was still well below the immigration rate of around 11.3‰.[[256]](#cite_note-264)[[257]](#cite_note-265) It is also noteworthy that the overwhelming majority of Portuguese emigrants tend to leave the country for short periods, with 56.8% of those having left the country in 2022 doing so for less than a year.[[258]](#cite_note-266)

Since the 1990s, along with a boom in [construction](/wiki/Economy_of_Portugal "Economy of Portugal"), several new waves of [Ukrainian](/wiki/Ukrainians_in_Portugal "Ukrainians in Portugal"), [Brazilian](/wiki/Brazilians_in_Portugal "Brazilians in Portugal"), [Afro-Portuguese](/wiki/Afro-Portuguese_people "Afro-Portuguese people") and other [Africans](/wiki/African_diaspora "African diaspora") have settled in the country. [Romanians](/wiki/Romanians_in_Portugal "Romanians in Portugal"), [Moldovans](/wiki/Moldovan_diaspora "Moldovan diaspora"), [Kosovo Albanians](/wiki/Kosovo_Albanians "Kosovo Albanians"), [Russians](/wiki/Russian_people "Russian people"), [Bulgarians](/wiki/Bulgarians_in_Portugal "Bulgarians in Portugal"), and [Chinese](/wiki/Chinese_people_in_Portugal "Chinese people in Portugal") have also migrated to the country. The numbers of [Venezuelan](/wiki/Venezuelan_people "Venezuelan people"), [Pakistani](/wiki/Pakistani_people "Pakistani people"), [Indian](/wiki/Indian_people "Indian people"), and [Bangladeshi](/wiki/Bangladeshi_people "Bangladeshi people") migrants are also significant. Moreover, Portugal's [Romani population](/wiki/Romani_people_in_Portugal "Romani people in Portugal") is estimated at 50,000.[[259]](#cite_note-267)

It is estimated that over 30,000 seasonal, often [illegal immigrants](/wiki/Illegal_immigrants "Illegal immigrants") work in [agriculture](/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"), mainly southern cities such as [Odemira](/wiki/Odemira "Odemira") where they are often exploited by organised seasonal workers' networks. These migrants, who frequently arrive without due documentation or work contracts, make up over 90% of agricultural workers in the south of Portugal. Most are Southeast Asians from India, [Bangladesh](/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh"), [Nepal](/wiki/Nepal "Nepal"), Pakistan and [Thailand](/wiki/Thailand "Thailand"). In the interior of the [Alentejo](/wiki/Alentejo "Alentejo") there are many African workers. Significant numbers also come from Eastern Europe, Moldova, Ukraine, Romania and Brazil.[[260]](#cite_note-268)

In addition, a significant number of [EU citizens](/wiki/EU_citizen "EU citizen"), mostly from [Italy](/wiki/Italy "Italy"), [France](/wiki/France "France"), [Germany](/wiki/Germany "Germany") or other northern European countries, have become permanent residents in the country.[[261]](#cite_note-269) There is also a large expatriate community made up of [Britons](/wiki/British_people "British people"), [Canadians](/wiki/Canadians "Canadians") and [people from the United States of America](/wiki/United_States "United States"). The British community is mostly composed of retired [pensioners](/wiki/Pensioner "Pensioner") who live in the [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve") and [Madeira](/wiki/Madeira "Madeira").

A National Statistics Institute (INE) study, conducted between 2022 and 2023, found out that 1.4 million people, (13% of the population) have immigrant background, in which 947,500 are first generation immigrants, concentrated mainly in the [Lisbon metropolitan area](/wiki/Lisbon_metropolitan_area "Lisbon metropolitan area") and the [Algarve](/wiki/Algarve "Algarve").[[244]](#cite_note-INERace-252) It is noteworthy that the survey was only carried out amongst people living legally in the country for at least one year at the time of the interview and that in 2022 the statistical office figures suggested that 16.1% of the country's population or 1,683,829 people were first generation immigrants.[[262]](#cite_note-270)[[263]](#cite_note-271)[[264]](#cite_note-272)

### Religion

Main article: [Religion in Portugal](/wiki/Religion_in_Portugal "Religion in Portugal")

Religion in Portugal (Census 2021)[[4]](#cite_note-Censos2021-5)

1. [Roman Catholicism](/wiki/Roman_Catholicism "Roman Catholicism") (80.2%)
2. [Protestantism](/wiki/Protestantism "Protestantism") (2.1%)
3. [Jehovah's Witnesses](/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses "Jehovah's Witnesses") (0.7%)
4. [Eastern Orthodoxy](/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy "Eastern Orthodoxy") (0.7%)
5. Other Christian (1%)
6. [Islam](/wiki/Islam "Islam") (0.4%)
7. [Hinduism](/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism") (0.2%)
8. [Buddhism](/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") (0.2%)
9. [Judaism](/wiki/Judaism "Judaism") (0.03%)
10. Other religion (0.3%)
11. [No religion](/wiki/Irreligious "Irreligious") (14.1%)

Roman Catholicism, which has a long history in Portugal, remains the dominant religion. Portugal has no official religion, though in the past, the [Catholic Church in Portugal](/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_Portugal "Catholic Church in Portugal") was the [state religion](/wiki/State_religion "State religion").[[265]](#cite_note-273)[[266]](#cite_note-274)

According to the 2021 Census, 80.2% of the Portuguese population was [Roman Catholic Christian](/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church").[[4]](#cite_note-Censos2021-5) The country has small Protestant, [Latter-day Saint](/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints "The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints"), [Muslim](/wiki/Islam_in_Portugal "Islam in Portugal"), [Hindu](/wiki/Hinduism_in_Portugal "Hinduism in Portugal"), [Sikh](/wiki/Sikh "Sikh"), [Eastern Orthodox Church](/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church "Eastern Orthodox Church"), [Jehovah's Witnesses](/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses "Jehovah's Witnesses"), [Baháʼí](/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%CA%BC%C3%AD_Faith "Baháʼí Faith"), [Buddhist](/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism"), [Jewish](/wiki/Judaism "Judaism") and [Spiritist](/wiki/Kardecist_spiritism "Kardecist spiritism") communities. Influences from [African Traditional Religion](/wiki/African_Traditional_Religion "African Traditional Religion") and Chinese Traditional Religion are also felt among many people, particularly in fields related with Traditional Chinese Medicine and Traditional African Herbal Medicine. Some 14.1% of the population declared themselves to be non-religious.[[4]](#cite_note-Censos2021-5)

Portugal is a [secular state](/wiki/Secular_state "Secular state"): [church and state were formally separated](/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state "Separation of church and state") during the First Portuguese Republic, and this was reiterated in the 1976 [Portuguese Constitution](/wiki/Portuguese_Constitution "Portuguese Constitution"). Other than the Constitution, the two most important documents relating to [religious freedom](/wiki/Religious_freedom "Religious freedom") in Portugal are the 1940 Concordata (later amended in 1971) between Portugal and the [Holy See](/wiki/Holy_See "Holy See") and the 2001 Religious Freedom Act. Many Portuguese holidays, festivals and traditions have a Christian origin or connotation.

### Languages

Main articles: [Languages of Portugal](/wiki/Languages_of_Portugal "Languages of Portugal") and [Portuguese language](/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language")

Portuguese is the official language of Portugal. [Mirandese](/wiki/Mirandese_language "Mirandese language") is also recognised as a co-official regional language in some municipalities of North-Eastern Portugal. It is part of the [Astur-Leonese](/wiki/Astur-Leonese "Astur-Leonese") group of languages.[[267]](#cite_note-275) An estimate of between 6,000 and 7,000 Mirandese speakers has been documented for Portugal.[[268]](#cite_note-276) Furthermore, a particular dialect known as [Barranquenho](/wiki/Barranquenho "Barranquenho"), spoken in [Barrancos](/wiki/Barrancos "Barrancos"), is also officially recognised and protected in Portugal since 2021.[[269]](#cite_note-277) [Minderico](/wiki/Minderico_language "Minderico language"), a [sociolect](/wiki/Sociolect "Sociolect") of the Portuguese language, is spoken by around 500 people in the town of [Minde](/wiki/Minde,_Portugal "Minde, Portugal").[[270]](#cite_note-278)

According to the [International English Proficiency Index](/wiki/EF_English_Proficiency_Index "EF English Proficiency Index"), Portugal has a high proficiency level in English, higher than those of other Romance-speaking European countries like France, Italy or Spain.[[271]](#cite_note-279)

### Education

Main article: [Education in Portugal](/wiki/Education_in_Portugal "Education in Portugal")

The educational system is divided into preschool (for those under age six), basic education (nine years, in three stages, compulsory), secondary education (three years, compulsory since 2010), and higher education (subdivided in university and [polytechnic](/wiki/Polytechnic_(Portugal) "Polytechnic (Portugal)") education). Universities are usually organised into [faculties](/wiki/Faculty_(division) "Faculty (division)"). Institutes and schools are also common designations for autonomous subdivisions of [Portuguese higher education institutions](/wiki/List_of_universities_and_colleges_in_Portugal "List of universities and colleges in Portugal").

The total adult literacy rate in Portugal was 99.8% in 2021.[[272]](#cite_note-280) According to the [Programme for International Student Assessment](/wiki/Programme_for_International_Student_Assessment "Programme for International Student Assessment") (PISA) 2018, Portugal scored around the [OECD](/wiki/OECD "OECD") average in reading, mathematics and science.[[273]](#cite_note-281)[[274]](#cite_note-282) In reading and mathematics, mean performance in 2018 was close to the level observed in 2009 to 2015; in science, mean performance in 2018 was below that of 2015, and returned close to the level observed in 2009 and 2012, near below average.[[275]](#cite_note-283)[[276]](#cite_note-284)[[277]](#cite_note-285)

About 47.6% of college-age citizens (20 years old) attend one of Portugal's higher education institutions[[278]](#cite_note-286)[[279]](#cite_note-287)[[280]](#cite_note-288) (compared with 50% in the United States and 35% in the OECD on average). In addition to being a destination for [international students](/wiki/International_student "International student"), Portugal is also among the top places of origin for international students. All higher education students, both domestic and international, totalled 380,937 in 2005.

Portuguese universities have existed since 1290. The [oldest Portuguese university](/wiki/University_of_Coimbra "University of Coimbra")[[281]](#cite_note-289) was first established in Lisbon before moving to [Coimbra](/wiki/Coimbra "Coimbra"). Historically, within the scope of the Portuguese Empire, the Portuguese founded the oldest engineering school of the [Americas](/wiki/Americas "Americas") (the *[Real Academia de Artilharia, Fortificação e Desenho](/wiki/Real_Academia_de_Artilharia,_Fortifica%C3%A7%C3%A3o_e_Desenho "Real Academia de Artilharia, Fortificação e Desenho")* of [Rio de Janeiro](/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro "Rio de Janeiro")) in 1792, as well as the oldest medical college in Asia (the *[Escola Médico-Cirúrgica](/wiki/Escola_M%C3%A9dico-Cir%C3%BArgica_de_Goa "Escola Médico-Cirúrgica de Goa")* of [Goa](/wiki/Goa "Goa")) in 1842. Presently, the largest university in Portugal is the [University of Lisbon](/wiki/University_of_Lisbon "University of Lisbon").

The [Bologna process](/wiki/Bologna_process "Bologna process") has been adopted by Portuguese universities and poly-technical institutes in 2006. Higher education in state-run educational establishments is provided on a competitive basis, a system of *[numerus clausus](/wiki/Numerus_clausus "Numerus clausus")* is enforced through a national database on student admissions. However, every higher education institution offers also a number of additional vacant places through other extraordinary admission processes for sportsmen, mature applicants (over 23 years old), [international students](/wiki/International_students "International students"), foreign students from the [Lusosphere](/wiki/Lusosphere "Lusosphere"), degree owners from other institutions, students from other institutions ([academic transfer](/wiki/Transfer_admissions_in_the_United_States "Transfer admissions in the United States")), former students (readmission), and course change, which are subject to specific standards and regulations set by each institution or course department.

Most student costs are supported with public money. Portugal has entered into [cooperation agreements with the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and other US institutions](/wiki/Higher_education_in_Portugal#International_partnership_agreements "Higher education in Portugal") to further develop and increase the effectiveness of Portuguese higher education and research.[[282]](#cite_note-Carvalho_et_al_2020-290)

### Health

Main article: [Health in Portugal](/wiki/Health_in_Portugal "Health in Portugal")

In 2025, Portugal ranked as 23rd best healthcare system in the world,[[283]](#cite_note-291) which was visibly lower than the previous [12th place in the 2000 World Health Organisation ranking of best public health systems in the world](/wiki/World_Health_Organization_ranking_of_health_systems_in_2000 "World Health Organization ranking of health systems in 2000"). The health system is characterised by three coexisting systems: the National Health Service (*Serviço Nacional de Saúde*, SNS), special social health insurance schemes for certain professions (health subsystems) and voluntary private health insurance. The SNS provides universal coverage. In addition, about 55% of the population is covered by the health subsystems,[[284]](#cite_note-292) 43% by private insurance schemes and another 12% by mutual funds.[[285]](#cite_note-293)[[286]](#cite_note-294)

The Ministry of Health is responsible for developing health policy as well as managing the SNS. Five regional health administrations are in charge of implementing the national health policy objectives, developing guidelines and protocols and supervising health care delivery. Decentralisation efforts have aimed at shifting financial and management responsibility to the regional level. In practice, the autonomy of regional health administrations over budget setting and spending has been limited to primary care. The SNS is predominantly funded through general taxation. Employer (including the state) and employee contributions represent the main funding sources of the health subsystems. In addition, direct payments by the patient and voluntary health insurance premiums account for a large proportion of funding.

Similarly to other 'Eur-A countries', (Western Europe), most Portuguese die from [noncommunicable diseases](/wiki/Noncommunicable_diseases "Noncommunicable diseases").[[287]](#cite_note-Saude-295) Mortality from [cardiovascular diseases](/wiki/Cardiovascular_diseases "Cardiovascular diseases") (CVD) is around 30,000 deaths per year, a third of all annual deaths,[[288]](#cite_note-296) but its two main components, ischaemic heart disease and cerebrovascular disease, display inverse trends compared with the Eur-A, with [cerebrovascular disease](/wiki/Cerebrovascular_disease "Cerebrovascular disease") being the single biggest killer in Portugal, with more than 11,000 deaths per year.[[289]](#cite_note-297) Oncological diseases represent 22% of all deaths in the country,[[287]](#cite_note-Saude-295) with lung and breast cancer cases being lower, and cervical and prostate cancer being more frequent. Diabetes mortality rates have been decreasing, from 4.5% in 2010 down to 2.8% in 2021.[[287]](#cite_note-Saude-295)

Portugal's [infant mortality rate](/wiki/Infant_mortality_rate "Infant mortality rate") (IMR) stood at 2,25 deaths per 1,000 live births as of 2024.[[290]](#cite_note-298) A Eurostat opinion-poll in 2023 found that 55.4% of adults rated their health as good or very good, the third lowest rate collected in the [European Union](/wiki/European_Union "European Union").[[291]](#cite_note-299) The largest university hospital in the country is [Hospital de Santa Maria](/wiki/Hospital_de_Santa_Maria "Hospital de Santa Maria"), Lisbon.[[292]](#cite_note-300)

Culture
-------

Main articles: [Culture of Portugal](/wiki/Culture_of_Portugal "Culture of Portugal") and [Mass media in Portugal](/wiki/Mass_media_in_Portugal "Mass media in Portugal")

Portugal has developed a specific culture while being influenced by various civilisations that have crossed the European continent and Mediterranean, or were introduced when it played an active role during the [Age of Discovery](/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Age_of_Discovery "Portugal in the Age of Discovery"). In the 1990s and 2000s (decade), Portugal modernised its public cultural facilities, in addition to the [Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation](/wiki/Calouste_Gulbenkian_Foundation "Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation") established in 1956 in Lisbon.

These include the [Belém Cultural Centre](/wiki/Bel%C3%A9m_Cultural_Centre "Belém Cultural Centre") in Lisbon, [Serralves Foundation](/wiki/Serralves_Foundation "Serralves Foundation") and the [Casa da Música](/wiki/Casa_da_M%C3%BAsica "Casa da Música"), both in [Porto](/wiki/Porto "Porto"), as well as new public cultural facilities like municipal libraries and concert halls that were built or renovated in many municipalities across the country. Portugal is home to [17](/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Portugal "List of World Heritage Sites in Portugal") UNESCO [World Heritage Sites](/wiki/World_Heritage_Site "World Heritage Site"), ranking it [9th in Europe and 18th in the world.](/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites_by_country "World Heritage Sites by country")

### Architecture

Main article: [Architecture of Portugal](/wiki/Architecture_of_Portugal "Architecture of Portugal")

Traditional architecture is distinctive and include the [Manueline](/wiki/Manueline "Manueline"), also known as Portuguese late [Gothic](/wiki/Gothic_architecture "Gothic architecture") a sumptuous, composite Portuguese style of architectural ornamentation of the first decades of the 16th century, followed by [Pombaline style](/wiki/Pombaline_style "Pombaline style") of the 18th century.

A 20th-century interpretation of traditional architecture, [Soft Portuguese style](/wiki/Soft_Portuguese_style "Soft Portuguese style"), appears extensively in major cities, especially Lisbon. Modern Portugal has given the world renowned architects like [Eduardo Souto de Moura](/wiki/Eduardo_Souto_de_Moura "Eduardo Souto de Moura"), [Álvaro Siza Vieira](/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_Siza_Vieira "Álvaro Siza Vieira") (both [Pritzker Prize](/wiki/Pritzker_Prize "Pritzker Prize") winners) and [Gonçalo Byrne](/wiki/Gon%C3%A7alo_Byrne "Gonçalo Byrne"). In Portugal [Tomás Taveira](/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Taveira "Tomás Taveira") is also noteworthy, particularly for stadium design.[[293]](#cite_note-301)[[294]](#cite_note-302) The [azulejo](/wiki/Azulejo "Azulejo") is a mainstream, typical element among Portugal's traditional building materials and construction techniques.[[295]](#cite_note-303)[[296]](#cite_note-304)

### Cuisine

Main articles: [Portuguese cuisine](/wiki/Portuguese_cuisine "Portuguese cuisine") and [Portuguese wine](/wiki/Portuguese_wine "Portuguese wine")

Portuguese cuisine is diverse, part of the [Atlantic diet](/wiki/Atlantic_diet "Atlantic diet"). A significant ingredient in the Portuguese diet is dry [cod](/wiki/Cod "Cod") (*[bacalhau](/wiki/Bacalhau "Bacalhau")* in Portuguese). They say that the Portuguese have mastered 365 different ways to prepare codfish, meaning a cod dish for each day of the year, to name a few [recipes](/wiki/Bacalhau#Bacalhau_dishes "Bacalhau"): [bacalhau à Brás](/wiki/Bacalhau_%C3%A0_Br%C3%A1s "Bacalhau à Brás"), [bacalhau à Gomes de Sá](/wiki/Bacalhau_%C3%A0_Gomes_de_S%C3%A1 "Bacalhau à Gomes de Sá"), [bacalhau com natas](/wiki/Bacalhau_com_natas "Bacalhau com natas"), bacalhau à Minhota, bacalhau espiritual, bacalhau à Lagareiro, bacalhau assado, [bacalhau à Zé do Pipo](/wiki/Bacalhau_%C3%A0_Z%C3%A9_do_Pipo "Bacalhau à Zé do Pipo"), [pastéis de bacalhau](/wiki/Past%C3%A9is_de_bacalhau "Pastéis de bacalhau"). Other fish recipes include grilled [sardines](/wiki/Sardines "Sardines"), [mackerel](/wiki/Mackerel "Mackerel"),[[297]](#cite_note-305) [sea bream](/wiki/Sea_bream "Sea bream"), and [caldeirada](/wiki/Caldeirada "Caldeirada"), a tomato-based [stew](/wiki/Stew "Stew") that can be made from several types of fish or shellfish, with a mix of onion, garlic, bay leaf, potatoes, peppers, parsley.[[298]](#cite_note-306)

Typical Portuguese meat recipes made out of the customary beef, pork, chicken, goat, lamb or duck meat, include *[cozido à portuguesa](/wiki/Cozido_%C3%A0_portuguesa "Cozido à portuguesa")*, *[feijoada](/wiki/Feijoada "Feijoada")*, *[frango de churrasco](/wiki/Frango_de_churrasco "Frango de churrasco")*, *leitão* (roast [suckling pig](/wiki/Suckling_pig "Suckling pig")), *[chanfana](/wiki/Chanfana "Chanfana")* and *[carne de porco à alentejana](/wiki/Carne_de_porco_%C3%A0_alentejana "Carne de porco à alentejana")*. Typical fast food dishes include the [Francesinha](/wiki/Francesinha "Francesinha") (Frenchie) from Porto and *bifanas* (grilled pork) or *prego* (grilled beef) [sandwiches](/wiki/Sandwich "Sandwich").[[299]](#cite_note-307) An egg custard tart pastry, the [pastel de nata](/wiki/Pastel_de_nata "Pastel de nata"), typical and popular among the Portuguese, became popular abroad and among foreign tourists visiting the country as well.[[300]](#cite_note-308)[[301]](#cite_note-309)

Portuguese wines have enjoyed recognition since the Romans, who associated Portugal with their god [Bacchus](/wiki/Dionysus "Dionysus"), due to its climate. Some of the best Portuguese wines are [Vinho Verde](/wiki/Vinho_Verde "Vinho Verde"), [Alvarinho](/wiki/Alvarinho "Alvarinho"), [Vinho do Douro](/wiki/Vinho_do_Douro "Vinho do Douro"), [Vinho do Alentejo](/wiki/Vinho_do_Alentejo "Vinho do Alentejo"), [Vinho do Dão](/wiki/Vinho_do_D%C3%A3o "Vinho do Dão"), [Vinho da Bairrada](/wiki/Vinho_da_Bairrada "Vinho da Bairrada") and the sweet [Port Wine](/wiki/Port_Wine "Port Wine"), [Madeira Wine](/wiki/Madeira_Wine "Madeira Wine"), and the [Moscatel](/wiki/Moscatel "Moscatel") from [Setúbal](/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal "Setúbal") and [Favaios](/wiki/Favaios "Favaios").

### Visual art

Main article: [Portuguese art](/wiki/Portuguese_art "Portuguese art")

Portugal has a [rich history in painting](/wiki/List_of_Portuguese_artists "List of Portuguese artists"). The first well-known painters dating back to the 15th century – like [Nuno Gonçalves](/wiki/Nuno_Gon%C3%A7alves "Nuno Gonçalves") and [Vasco Fernandes](/wiki/Gr%C3%A3o_Vasco "Grão Vasco") – were part of the late Gothic painting period.[[302]](#cite_note-310)[[303]](#cite_note-311) During the Renaissance, Portuguese painting was highly influenced by Northern European painting.[[304]](#cite_note-312) In the Baroque period [Josefa de Óbidos](/wiki/Josefa_de_%C3%93bidos "Josefa de Óbidos") and [Vieira Lusitano](/wiki/Vieira_Lusitano "Vieira Lusitano") were the most prolific painters.[[305]](#cite_note-313)
[José Malhoa](/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Malhoa "José Malhoa"), known for his work *Fado*, and [Columbano Bordalo Pinheiro](/wiki/Columbano_Bordalo_Pinheiro "Columbano Bordalo Pinheiro") (who painted the portraits of [Teófilo Braga](/wiki/Te%C3%B3filo_Braga "Teófilo Braga") and [Antero de Quental](/wiki/Antero_de_Quental "Antero de Quental")) were both references in [naturalist painting](/wiki/Naturalist_school_of_painting "Naturalist school of painting").[[306]](#cite_note-314)[[307]](#cite_note-315)

The 20th century saw the arrival of [Modernism](/wiki/Modernism "Modernism"), and along with it came the most prominent Portuguese painters: [Amadeo de Souza-Cardoso](/wiki/Amadeo_de_Souza-Cardoso "Amadeo de Souza-Cardoso"), who was heavily influenced by French painters, particularly the Delaunays ([Robert](/wiki/Robert_Delaunay "Robert Delaunay") and [Sonia](/wiki/Sonia_Delaunay "Sonia Delaunay")).[[308]](#cite_note-316) Among his best-known works is *Canção Popular – a Russa e o Fígaro*. Other great modernist painters/writers include [Carlos Botelho](/wiki/Carlos_Botelho "Carlos Botelho") and [Almada Negreiros](/wiki/Almada_Negreiros "Almada Negreiros"), friend to the poet [Fernando Pessoa](/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa "Fernando Pessoa"), who painted Pessoa's portrait. He was deeply influenced by both [Cubist](/wiki/Cubist "Cubist") and [Futurist](/wiki/Futurism_(art) "Futurism (art)") trends.[[309]](#cite_note-317)[[310]](#cite_note-318)

Prominent international figures in visual arts today include painters [Vieira da Silva](/wiki/Vieira_da_Silva "Vieira da Silva"),[[311]](#cite_note-319) [Júlio Pomar](/wiki/J%C3%BAlio_Pomar "Júlio Pomar"),[[312]](#cite_note-320) [Joana Vasconcelos](/wiki/Joana_Vasconcelos "Joana Vasconcelos"),[[313]](#cite_note-321) [Julião Sarmento](/wiki/Juli%C3%A3o_Sarmento "Julião Sarmento")[[314]](#cite_note-322) and [Paula Rego](/wiki/Paula_Rego "Paula Rego").[[315]](#cite_note-323)

### Literature

Main article: [Portuguese literature](/wiki/Portuguese_literature "Portuguese literature")

Portuguese literature, one of the earliest Western literatures, developed through text as well as song. Until 1350, the [Portuguese-Galician](/wiki/Portuguese-Galician "Portuguese-Galician") [troubadours](/wiki/Troubadour "Troubadour") spread their literary influence to most of the Iberian Peninsula,[[316]](#cite_note-324) like King [D. Dinis](/wiki/Denis_of_Portugal "Denis of Portugal") (1261–1325) who became famous for his poetry. Other kings would write and sponsor works of literature across Portuguese history, like [D. Fernando](/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Portugal "Ferdinand I of Portugal") (1367–1383) who supported Pêro Menino in writing o *Livro da Falcoaria*.[[64]](#cite_note-Carvalho_2014-72)

Adventurer and poet [Luís de Camões](/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_de_Cam%C3%B5es "Luís de Camões") (c. 1524–1580) wrote the epic poem *[Os Lusíadas](/wiki/Os_Lus%C3%ADadas "Os Lusíadas")* (*The Lusiads*), with [Virgil](/wiki/Virgil "Virgil")'s *[Aeneid](/wiki/Aeneid "Aeneid")* as his main influence.[[317]](#cite_note-WDL-325) Modern Portuguese poetry is rooted in neoclassic and contemporary styles, as exemplified by [Bocage](/wiki/Manuel_Maria_Barbosa_du_Bocage "Manuel Maria Barbosa du Bocage") (1765–1805), [Antero de Quental](/wiki/Antero_de_Quental "Antero de Quental") (1842–1891) and [Fernando Pessoa](/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa "Fernando Pessoa") (1888–1935). Modern Portuguese literature is represented by authors such as [Almeida Garrett](/wiki/Almeida_Garrett "Almeida Garrett"), [Camilo Castelo Branco](/wiki/Camilo_Castelo_Branco "Camilo Castelo Branco"), [Eça de Queirós](/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Maria_de_E%C3%A7a_de_Queir%C3%B3s "José Maria de Eça de Queirós"), [Fernando Pessoa](/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa "Fernando Pessoa"), [Sophia de Mello Breyner Andresen](/wiki/Sophia_de_Mello_Breyner_Andresen "Sophia de Mello Breyner Andresen"), [António Lobo Antunes](/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Lobo_Antunes "António Lobo Antunes"), [Miguel Torga](/wiki/Miguel_Torga "Miguel Torga") and [Agustina Bessa-Luís](/wiki/Agustina_Bessa-Lu%C3%ADs "Agustina Bessa-Luís"). Particularly popular and distinguished is [José Saramago](/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Saramago "José Saramago"), recipient of the 1998 [Nobel Prize in Literature](/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature "Nobel Prize in Literature").

### Music

Main article: [Music of Portugal](/wiki/Music_of_Portugal "Music of Portugal")

The Music of Portugal encompasses a wide variety of genres. The traditional one is the Portuguese folk music which has deep roots in local customs, utilising instruments such as [bagpipes](/wiki/Bagpipes "Bagpipes") (*[gaita](/wiki/Gaita_(bagpipe) "Gaita (bagpipe)")*),[[318]](#cite_note-326) drums, flutes, tambourines, accordions and ukuleles (*[cavaquinho](/wiki/Cavaquinho "Cavaquinho")*).[[319]](#cite_note-327) Within Portuguese folk music is the renowned genre of [Fado](/wiki/Fado "Fado"), a melancholic urban music originated in [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon") in the 19th century, probably inside bohemian environments, usually associated with the [Portuguese guitar](/wiki/Portuguese_guitar "Portuguese guitar") and *saudade*, or longing.[[320]](#cite_note-328) [Coimbra fado](/wiki/Coimbra_fado "Coimbra fado"), a unique type of "[troubadour](/wiki/Troubadour "Troubadour") serenading" fado, is also noteworthy. Internationally notable performers include [Amália Rodrigues](/wiki/Am%C3%A1lia_Rodrigues "Amália Rodrigues"), [Carlos Paredes](/wiki/Carlos_Paredes "Carlos Paredes"), [José Afonso](/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Afonso "José Afonso"), [Mariza](/wiki/Mariza "Mariza"), [Carlos do Carmo](/wiki/Carlos_do_Carmo "Carlos do Carmo"), [António Chainho](/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Chainho "António Chainho"), [Mísia](/wiki/M%C3%ADsia "Mísia"), [Dulce Pontes](/wiki/Dulce_Pontes "Dulce Pontes") and [Madredeus](/wiki/Madredeus "Madredeus").[[321]](#cite_note-329)[[322]](#cite_note-330)

In addition to [Folk](/wiki/Folk_music "Folk music"), Fado and Classical music, other genres are present at Portugal like pop and other types of modern music, particularly from North America and the United Kingdom, as well as a wide range of Portuguese, Caribbean, Lusophone African and Brazilian [artists and bands](/wiki/List_of_Portuguese_bands "List of Portuguese bands"). Artists with international recognition include [Dulce Pontes](/wiki/Dulce_Pontes "Dulce Pontes"),[[323]](#cite_note-331) [Moonspell](/wiki/Moonspell "Moonspell"),[[324]](#cite_note-332) [Buraka Som Sistema](/wiki/Buraka_Som_Sistema "Buraka Som Sistema"),[[325]](#cite_note-333) [Blasted Mechanism](/wiki/Blasted_Mechanism "Blasted Mechanism"),[[326]](#cite_note-334) [David Carreira](/wiki/David_Carreira "David Carreira") and [The Gift](/wiki/The_Gift_(band) "The Gift (band)"),[[327]](#cite_note-335) with the three latter being nominees for a [MTV Europe Music Award](/wiki/MTV_Europe_Music_Award "MTV Europe Music Award").[[328]](#cite_note-336)[[329]](#cite_note-337)

Portugal has several summer music festivals, such as *[Festival Sudoeste](/wiki/Festival_Sudoeste "Festival Sudoeste")* in [Zambujeira do Mar](/wiki/Zambujeira_do_Mar "Zambujeira do Mar"),[[330]](#cite_note-338) *[Festival de Paredes de Coura](/wiki/Festival_de_Paredes_de_Coura "Festival de Paredes de Coura")* in [Paredes de Coura](/wiki/Paredes_de_Coura "Paredes de Coura"),[[331]](#cite_note-339) *[Festival Vilar de Mouros](/wiki/Festival_Vilar_de_Mouros "Festival Vilar de Mouros")* near [Caminha](/wiki/Caminha "Caminha"), *[Boom Festival](/wiki/Boom_Festival "Boom Festival")* in [Idanha-a-Nova municipality](/wiki/Idanha-a-Nova "Idanha-a-Nova"), *[NOS Alive](/wiki/NOS_Alive "NOS Alive")*, *Sumol Summer Fest* in [Ericeira](/wiki/Ericeira "Ericeira"), *[Rock in Rio Lisboa](/wiki/Rock_in_Rio#Lisboa,_Portugal,_and_Madrid,_Spain "Rock in Rio")*[[332]](#cite_note-340) and *[Super Bock Super Rock](/wiki/Super_Bock_Super_Rock "Super Bock Super Rock")* in [Greater Lisbon](/wiki/Grande_Lisboa "Grande Lisboa"), plus [*Primavera Sound Porto*](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primavera_Sound_(Portugal) "pt:Primavera Sound (Portugal)") and [*MEO Marés Vivas*](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/MEO_Mar%C3%A9s_Vivas "pt:MEO Marés Vivas") in the [Greater Porto area](/wiki/Grande_Porto "Grande Porto").[[333]](#cite_note-341)

[Luísa](/wiki/Lu%C3%ADsa_Sobral "Luísa Sobral") and [Salvador Sobral](/wiki/Salvador_Sobral "Salvador Sobral") after winning the [Eurovision Song Contest 2017](/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2017 "Eurovision Song Contest 2017").

The student festivals of *[Queima das Fitas](/wiki/Queima_das_Fitas "Queima das Fitas"),* which are major events in a number of cities across Portugal, show every year a selection of well-established, high-profile musicians and bands to the public as well as newer, on the rise, upcoming success artists seeking definite recognition.[[334]](#cite_note-342) In 2005, Portugal held the [MTV Europe Music Awards](/wiki/MTV_Europe_Music_Awards "MTV Europe Music Awards"), in [Pavilhão Atlântico](/wiki/Pavilh%C3%A3o_Atl%C3%A2ntico "Pavilhão Atlântico"), [Lisbon](/wiki/Lisbon "Lisbon").[[335]](#cite_note-343) Furthermore, Portugal won the [Eurovision Song Contest 2017](/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2017 "Eurovision Song Contest 2017") in [Kyiv](/wiki/Kyiv "Kyiv") with the song "[Amar pelos dois](/wiki/Amar_pelos_dois "Amar pelos dois")" presented by [Salvador Sobral](/wiki/Salvador_Sobral "Salvador Sobral"), and subsequently hosted the [2018 contest](/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_2018 "Eurovision Song Contest 2018").[[336]](#cite_note-344)[[337]](#cite_note-345)

Portuguese classical music constitutes an important chapter of Western music.[[338]](#cite_note-346) Over the centuries, names of composers and performers have stood out, such as the troubadours [Martim Codax](/wiki/Martim_Codax "Martim Codax")[[339]](#cite_note-347) and [King Denis I](/wiki/Denis_of_Portugal "Denis of Portugal"),[[340]](#cite_note-348) the polyphonists [Duarte Lobo](/wiki/Duarte_Lobo "Duarte Lobo"),[[341]](#cite_note-349) [Filipe de Magalhães](/wiki/Filipe_de_Magalh%C3%A3es "Filipe de Magalhães"), [Manuel Cardoso](/wiki/Manuel_Cardoso_(composer) "Manuel Cardoso (composer)")[[342]](#cite_note-350) and [Pedro de Cristo](/wiki/Pedro_de_Cristo "Pedro de Cristo"), the organist [Manuel Rodrigues Coelho](/wiki/Manuel_Rodrigues_Coelho "Manuel Rodrigues Coelho"), the composer and harpsichordist [Carlos Seixas](/wiki/Carlos_Seixas "Carlos Seixas"),[[343]](#cite_note-351) the singer [Luísa Todi](/wiki/Lu%C3%ADsa_Todi "Luísa Todi"),[[344]](#cite_note-352) symphonist and pianist [João Domingos Bomtempo](/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Domingos_Bomtempo "João Domingos Bomtempo")[[345]](#cite_note-353) or composer and musicologist [Fernando Lopes Graça](/wiki/Fernando_Lopes_Gra%C3%A7a "Fernando Lopes Graça").[[346]](#cite_note-354) The golden period of Portuguese music coincided, arguably, with the heyday of classical polyphony in the 17th century (Escola de [Évora](/wiki/%C3%89vora "Évora"), [Santa Cruz de Coimbra](/wiki/Santa_Cruz_de_Coimbra "Santa Cruz de Coimbra")). Among the great current references, the names of pianists [Artur Pizarro](/wiki/Artur_Pizarro "Artur Pizarro"),[[347]](#cite_note-355) [Maria João Pires](/wiki/Maria_Jo%C3%A3o_Pires "Maria João Pires")[[348]](#cite_note-356) and [Sequeira Costa](/wiki/Sequeira_Costa "Sequeira Costa"),[[349]](#cite_note-357) violinist [Carlos Damas](/wiki/Carlos_Damas "Carlos Damas"),[[350]](#cite_note-358) composer [Emmanuel Nunes](/wiki/Emmanuel_Nunes "Emmanuel Nunes"),[[351]](#cite_note-359) composer and conductor [Álvaro Cassuto](/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_Cassuto "Álvaro Cassuto")[[352]](#cite_note-360) stand out. The most important symphony orchestras are the [Fundação Gulbenkian](/wiki/Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Gulbenkian "Fundação Gulbenkian"),[[353]](#cite_note-361) the Porto National Orchestra and the Portuguese Symphony Orchestra.[[354]](#cite_note-362) When it comes to opera, the Teatro Nacional de São Carlos in Lisbon is the most representative.[[355]](#cite_note-363)

### Sport

Main article: [Sport in Portugal](/wiki/Sport_in_Portugal "Sport in Portugal")

[Football](/wiki/Association_football "Association football") is the most popular sport in Portugal. There are several football competitions ranging from local amateur to world-class professional level. All-time greats [Eusébio](/wiki/Eus%C3%A9bio "Eusébio"), [Figo](/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Figo "Luís Figo") and [Cristiano Ronaldo](/wiki/Cristiano_Ronaldo "Cristiano Ronaldo") are major symbols of [Portuguese football](/wiki/Portuguese_football "Portuguese football") history.[[356]](#cite_note-364)[[357]](#cite_note-365) Portuguese football managers are also noteworthy, with [José Mourinho](/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mourinho "José Mourinho")[[358]](#cite_note-366)[[359]](#cite_note-367) and [Abel Ferreira](/wiki/Abel_Ferreira "Abel Ferreira")[[360]](#cite_note-368)[[361]](#cite_note-369) among the most renowned.

The [Portugal national football team](/wiki/Portugal_national_football_team "Portugal national football team") has won one [UEFA European Championship](/wiki/UEFA_European_Championship "UEFA European Championship") title: the [UEFA Euro 2016](/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2016 "UEFA Euro 2016"), with a 1–0 victory in the [final](/wiki/UEFA_Euro_2016_Final "UEFA Euro 2016 Final") over [France](/wiki/France_national_football_team "France national football team"), the tournament hosts. In addition, Portugal finished first in the [2018–19 UEFA Nations League](/wiki/2018%E2%80%9319_UEFA_Nations_League "2018–19 UEFA Nations League"), second in the [Euro 2004](/wiki/2004_European_Football_Championship "2004 European Football Championship"), third in the [1966 FIFA World Cup](/wiki/1966_FIFA_World_Cup "1966 FIFA World Cup") and fourth in the [2006 FIFA World Cup](/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup "2006 FIFA World Cup"). At youth level, Portugal have won two [FIFA World Youth Championships](/wiki/FIFA_World_Youth_Championship "FIFA World Youth Championship").

[S.L. Benfica](/wiki/S.L._Benfica "S.L. Benfica"), [Sporting CP](/wiki/Sporting_CP "Sporting CP") and [FC Porto](/wiki/FC_Porto "FC Porto") are the largest [sports clubs](/wiki/Sports_clubs "Sports clubs") by popularity and number of trophies, often known as "*os três grandes*" (["the big three"](/wiki/Big_Three_(Portugal) "Big Three (Portugal)")). They have won eight titles in the European [UEFA](/wiki/UEFA "UEFA") club competitions and present in 21 finals. The [Portuguese Football Federation](/wiki/Portuguese_Football_Federation "Portuguese Football Federation") (FPF) – *Federação Portuguesa de Futebol* – annually hosts the [Algarve Cup](/wiki/Algarve_Cup "Algarve Cup"), a [women's football](/wiki/Women%27s_association_football "Women's association football") tournament.

Other than football, many Portuguese sports clubs, including the "big three", compete in other sports events with a varying level of success and popularity, these include [roller hockey](/wiki/Roller_hockey_(quad) "Roller hockey (quad)"), [basketball](/wiki/Basketball "Basketball"), [futsal](/wiki/Futsal "Futsal"), [handball](/wiki/Team_handball "Team handball"), [volleyball](/wiki/Volleyball "Volleyball") and [athletics](/wiki/Sport_of_athletics "Sport of athletics"). The [Portuguese national rugby union team](/wiki/Portugal_national_rugby_union_team "Portugal national rugby union team") qualified for the [2027 Rugby World Cup](/wiki/2027_Rugby_World_Cup "2027 Rugby World Cup") and the [Portuguese national rugby sevens team](/wiki/Portugal_national_rugby_sevens_team "Portugal national rugby sevens team") has played in the [World Rugby Sevens Series](/wiki/World_Rugby_Sevens_Series "World Rugby Sevens Series").

[Road cycling](/wiki/Road_bicycle_racing "Road bicycle racing"), with [Volta a Portugal](/wiki/Volta_a_Portugal "Volta a Portugal") the most important race, is a popular sports event and includes professional cycling teams such as [Sporting CP](/wiki/Sporting_CP "Sporting CP"), [Boavista](/wiki/Boavista_(cycling_team) "Boavista (cycling team)"), [Clube de Ciclismo de Tavira](/wiki/Clube_de_Ciclismo_de_Tavira "Clube de Ciclismo de Tavira") and [União Ciclista da Maia](/wiki/Uni%C3%A3o_Ciclista_da_Maia "União Ciclista da Maia"). In motorsport, Portugal is noted for the [Rally of Portugal](/wiki/Rally_of_Portugal "Rally of Portugal"), and the [Estoril](/wiki/Estoril_Circuit "Estoril Circuit") and [Algarve Circuits](/wiki/Algarve_Circuit "Algarve Circuit") as well as the revived [Porto Street Circuit](/wiki/Porto_Street_Circuit "Porto Street Circuit") which holds a stage of the WTCC every two years, as well as for a number of internationally noted racers such as [Miguel Oliveira](/wiki/Miguel_Oliveira "Miguel Oliveira"), [Tiago Monteiro](/wiki/Tiago_Monteiro "Tiago Monteiro"), [António Félix da Costa](/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_F%C3%A9lix_da_Costa "António Félix da Costa"), [Filipe Albuquerque](/wiki/Filipe_Albuquerque "Filipe Albuquerque"), [Pedro Lamy](/wiki/Pedro_Lamy "Pedro Lamy"), [Armindo Araújo](/wiki/Armindo_Ara%C3%BAjo "Armindo Araújo") and others in a range of varied [motorsports](/wiki/Motorsports "Motorsports").

In water, Portugal has three major sports: [swimming](/wiki/Swimming_(sport) "Swimming (sport)"), [water polo](/wiki/Water_polo "Water polo") and [surfing](/wiki/Surfing "Surfing"). Portugal had success in [canoeing](/wiki/Canoeing "Canoeing") with several world and European champions, such as olympic medalist [Fernando Pimenta](/wiki/Fernando_Pimenta "Fernando Pimenta"). Annually, the country hosts one of the stages of the [World Surf League](/wiki/World_Surf_League "World Surf League") men's and women's *Championship Tour*, the [MEO Rip Curl Pro Portugal](/wiki/MEO_Rip_Curl_Pro_Portugal "MEO Rip Curl Pro Portugal") at the [Supertubos](/wiki/Supertubos "Supertubos") in [Peniche](/wiki/Peniche,_Portugal "Peniche, Portugal"). Northern Portugal has its own original [martial art](/wiki/Martial_art "Martial art"), *[Jogo do Pau](/wiki/Jogo_do_Pau "Jogo do Pau")*, in which fighters use staffs to confront one or several opponents. Other popular sport-related recreational outdoor activities include [airsoft](/wiki/Airsoft "Airsoft"), fishing, [golf](/wiki/Golf "Golf"), hiking, hunting and [orienteering](/wiki/Orienteering "Orienteering"). Portugal is one of the world's leading [golf](/wiki/Golf "Golf") destinations.[[362]](#cite_note-370)[[363]](#cite_note-371)
